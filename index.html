The Numbers Football Cannot Ignore

Colour blindness is easy to miss because many children do not know they see differently. Football is easy to misjudge because the mistake usually looks like a football problem before anyone checks whether the picture was clear.

Around 1 in 12 males are commonly estimated to have colour vision deficiency.

Around 1 in 200 females are commonly estimated to have colour vision deficiency.

Girls can be colour blind too. The statistic is smaller, but the football barrier is not smaller when a girl is affected.

A 2025 paediatric systematic review and meta-analysis estimated colour vision deficiency at around 2.59% of children and adolescents overall.

The same review estimated higher prevalence in males, around 4.38%, and lower prevalence in females, around 0.64%.

Based on global child-population estimates and the 2.59% pooled prevalence figure, roughly 60–63 million children worldwide may have congenital colour vision deficiency. This is a scale estimate, not an official count.

Using UK under-18 population estimates and common sex-specific prevalence assumptions, roughly 634,000–636,000 UK under-18s may be affected. This is an author estimate, not an official count.

In an average co-educational classroom, there may be at least one colour-blind child. This is a teacher-facing rule of thumb, not a census fact.

In a typical male football squad, guidance commonly suggests there may statistically be at least one colour-blind player.

A standard eyesight check and a colour vision test are not always the same thing, so parents may need to ask specifically about colour vision testing.

A child can know colour names and still struggle to use colour quickly enough in football.

A player can pass a normal eyesight chart and still have a colour vision issue if colour vision is not specifically tested.

Football uses colour everywhere: kits, bibs, cones, balls, lines, goalkeeper shirts, referee shirts, tactical boards, apps, screens and coaching instructions.

A colour-blind player may spend mental energy checking colours all game. That can reduce focus, slow decisions, create doubt and waste energy the player should be using to play football.

The strongest practical lesson is simple: do not rely on colour alone when something important must be understood quickly.

This is not a rare excuse. It is a hidden design problem.

And design problems can be changed.

Before You Blame the Player

A child can love football with everything they have and still make a mistake that looks obvious to everyone else. They can train hard, listen carefully, practise in the garden until the light disappears, and dream about becoming a professional footballer with the kind of seriousness adults sometimes mistake for childhood fantasy. They can want it badly enough to give up weekends, school nights, free time, comfort and confidence. They can do all of that, and still, in the middle of a match, pass the ball to the wrong shirt.

From the sideline, that mistake is easy to explain. It looks like poor awareness. It looks like hesitation. It looks like a player who was not concentrating, a child who should have scanned earlier, a footballer who needs to move the ball quicker. The coach sees the pass. The teammates see the pass. The parents see the pass. The opponent sees the chance. Everybody sees the outcome, but almost nobody sees the moment before it.

That moment before the mistake is where this book begins. It is the moment where two shirts did not separate quickly enough. It is the moment where socks were muddy, shadows cut across the pitch, the goalkeeper kit looked too close to an outfield shirt, or a bib that seemed bright in the coach’s hand became unclear once ten players started moving at speed. It is the moment where the player needed one more check, but football did not give them time. The ball arrived, the pressure arrived, the decision arrived, and the picture was not certain.

Football does not wait for certainty. The passing lane closes, the shout comes, the label forms, and the child remembers. If that kind of moment happens once, people call it a mistake. If it happens again, people start calling it a pattern. If it happens often enough, people start calling it the player. He is too slow. She panics under pressure. He does not scan. She is not switched on. He has not got the football brain. She is not brave enough. He is not ready for this level.

Sometimes those explanations may be right. Sometimes a bad pass is just a bad pass. Sometimes the player did need to scan earlier, choose better, communicate sooner or improve technically. This book is not here to turn every football mistake into colour blindness. That would be dishonest, and it would not help players. Football still has to be coached. Standards still matter. Players still need responsibility, courage, discipline, speed and skill.

But it is also dishonest to assume every player is seeing the same picture. Football makes that assumption constantly. It assumes the kits are clear, the bibs are obvious, the cones stand out, the lines can be seen, the goalkeeper is easy to separate, and the referee shirt is not part of the confusion. It assumes colour can be used instantly, under pressure, while a child is running, tired, nervous, being shouted at, trying not to make the same mistake again, and making decisions faster than they can explain them.

For many players, that assumption works. For some players, it does not. When it does not, the cost is not only a misplaced pass. The deeper cost is what the player starts to believe about themselves. A child who keeps being blamed for moments they cannot explain does not usually think, “Maybe the visual information was unclear.” A child thinks, “Maybe I am slow. Maybe I panic. Maybe I am not clever enough. Maybe I am not good enough. Maybe football is not for me.”

That is the real danger. Not just the wrong pass, but the wrong explanation. Not just the mistake, but the identity that grows from being misunderstood. A player can survive a bad touch. A player can recover from a poor decision. What is harder to recover from is years of being told the problem is attitude, intelligence, bravery or effort when part of the problem may have been the picture.

This is why colour blindness in football matters. Not because colour blindness makes someone bad at football. Not because colour-blind players cannot succeed. Not because this book is asking coaches to lower standards. It is not. Football should demand focus, communication, scanning, decision-making, courage and speed. But standards only mean something when the test is fair. If a child is being judged inside unclear information, the judgement may be wrong.

When the setup is confusing, a player can look worse than they are. They may hold the ball too long because they are confirming who is safe. They may play backwards because the forward pass feels visually risky. They may stop asking for the ball because one wrong-team pass has already hurt enough. They may look nervous, but the nervousness may come from experience. They may not fear football itself. They may fear the moment when the colours lie again and everyone thinks the mistake tells the truth.

That fear can become exhausting. A player who should be thinking about space may be thinking about shirts. A player who should be scanning pressure may be checking socks. A player who should be trusting instinct may be asking, “Is that my teammate or the opponent?” A player who should be in flow may be trapped in doubt. Over one pass, that doubt may look tiny. Over a full match, it can become a hidden workload. The player is not only playing football. They are spending energy trying to prove the picture is safe before they trust it.

Parents can misread that workload too. A parent may spend years paying for football: subs, boots, travel, hotels, tournaments, private coaching, trial fees, missed work days, weekends away from home and family time that never comes back. They may arrange school nights, holidays and household routines around the dream. They may watch their child chase professional football with everything they have, only to discover years later that nobody had checked whether the game itself was always clear enough for the child to read.

That discovery can bring relief, but it can also bring grief. Not because colour blindness alone proves why a dream failed. Football dreams fail for many reasons: talent, timing, injury, selection, coaching, money, confidence, opportunity, luck, growth and the brutal difficulty of becoming professional. But late discovery leaves a painful question behind. What if we had known earlier? What if the player had learned to use numbers sooner? What if teammates had called names earlier? What if a coach had changed the bibs? What if the parent had asked for a colour vision test? What if the club had checked kit contrast before the trial? What if the child had not spent years thinking the problem was them?

Some adaptations are simple. Numbers can help. Names can help. Positions can help. Voice cues can help. Socks, shorts, movement patterns, greyscale kit checks, clearer cones, better bib contrast and private conversations can all help. But simple does not mean small. Sometimes a small change arrives too late to protect confidence, too late to rebuild trust, too late to give a player the chance they might have had if they had understood their own vision earlier.

This book exists so that help can arrive earlier. Earlier for the player who is still chasing the dream. Earlier for the parent who has not yet connected the signs. Earlier for the coach who has seen the mistake but not the hidden picture. Earlier for the club that can make the setup clearer before another child is judged unfairly. Earlier for the school using colour-coded cones. Earlier for the academy trial where one unclear moment can change how a player is seen. Earlier for the next Number 12.

This book is not here to diagnose children from the touchline. A child struggling with certain kits is not proof. A wrong pass is not proof. A missed cone is not proof. A quiet car journey after a bad game is not proof. But repeated patterns deserve attention. If the struggle appears when colours, kits, bibs, cones, shadows, mud, floodlights or visual pressure increase, the better question is not only, “Why did you do that?” The better question is, “What did you see?”

That question can change everything. It can turn blame into curiosity, shame into information, confusion into a plan, and a hidden problem into something football can actually fix. The player may not be lazy. The player may not be stupid. The player may not lack football intelligence. The player may be trying to play a fast, moving, colour-coded game inside a picture nobody else has thought to check.

Before you blame the player, check the picture. Then make the game visible. Then judge the football.

Prologue — Number 12

There was a boy who wanted to become a footballer. Not a boy who liked football for a season, or only played because his friends played, or enjoyed the game in the easy way children enjoy something before the next interest arrives. This was different. Football lived in him. It followed him through school, through the walk home, through dinner, through the evenings when other children went inside and he stayed in the garden with a ball at his feet.

He did not love watching football as much as he loved playing it. Watching was too far away. He wanted the ball close enough to feel. He wanted the wall pass, the first touch, the turn, the shot, the small private victory of controlling a ball that came back awkwardly off the shed. In the small back garden, he kicked it again and again. Left foot, right foot, control, pass, control, shoot. Sometimes the ball came back clean. Sometimes it hit a stone, a plant pot or a broken bit of ground and bounced wrong. He adjusted, because that was what football asked him to do. Adjust, react, try again.

In his head, the dream was enormous. He imagined stadiums, crowds and the feeling of becoming the best in the world. Adults may smile at a child’s dream like that, but children do not always know they are supposed to make their dreams smaller. To him, it was not a hobby. It was a future. He did not simply want to play. He wanted to become.

He wore number 12. Number 12 was not usually the star. Number 12 was close enough to the team to believe, close enough to warm up, close enough to be needed, close enough to be seen, but often not close enough to be trusted. That number became more than a shirt. It became a feeling: almost in, almost picked, almost believed, almost understood.

The confusing part was that he could train well. In small spaces, when the drill was clear and the players were easy to separate, he could look sharp. He had a good touch. He could move the ball. He could turn. He could see passes. He could play with rhythm when the picture made sense. There were moments that gave him just enough evidence to keep believing. Moments where he looked like the player he imagined he could become.

Then match day came, and something changed. Not every match, which made it harder to understand. Some games were fine. Some kits stood apart. Some days the light helped. Some opponents were easy to read. Some pitches made the picture clearer. In those games, he could play closer to himself. But other matches felt different before he had the language to explain why. Shirts seemed closer together. Socks got muddy. The low winter light flattened the colours. The pitch turned dark in patches. Bodies moved through shadows. The game became crowded, quick and uncertain.

The ball would arrive and the decision would be waiting. Football decisions do not politely stand still. They come with pressure, movement, noise and consequence. A teammate checks short. An opponent steps in. A runner goes. A parent shouts. A coach shouts something else. The player has half a breath to decide. In that moment, Number 12 checked. Just a little. Just enough to cost him.

Is that my teammate? Is that the opponent? Are those socks ours? Is the shirt darker because of mud? Is he showing for the ball or closing me down? Can I trust this pass? Most people did not see those questions. They saw the delay. They saw the pass arrive late. They saw the wrong shirt receive it. They saw the attack break down. Then came the sound he knew too well.

“Wake up.”

“Move it quicker.”

“You have to see that.”

Number 12 heard those words in his body before he heard them properly in his ears. He knew what they meant. They meant people had already decided what the mistake said about him. Slow. Nervous. Not sharp. Not brave. Not switched on. Not good enough. The strange thing was that he cared so much. That was what nobody saw. He cared so much that every mistake hurt twice: once because the football went wrong, and again because everyone thought the mistake explained him.

He did not know how to defend himself. What could he say? That the shirts looked similar? That he was not sure? That he needed another look? That the colours changed when the players moved? That the socks were muddy? That the cone disappeared? That he did not know why it kept happening? Those sentences did not sound like football answers. They sounded like excuses. So he stayed quiet, because silence was easier than trying to explain something he did not understand himself.

Number 12 did not know he was colour blind. He did not know his eyesight was also part of the problem. He did not know that sharpness in football is not only about muscles, attitude and bravery. It is also about information: what you see, how quickly you separate it, how much you trust it and how much energy it costs before you act. He thought everyone saw the game like he did, because a child has no second version of vision to compare with. A child does not walk onto the pitch thinking, “My picture may be different from everyone else’s.” A child usually thinks, “This is football.”

So he tried to fix the problem the only way he knew. He tried harder. He concentrated more. He told himself to be quicker, braver, calmer, sharper. He told himself not to panic. He told himself not to mess up. But trying harder did not always solve it. Sometimes it made the worry louder. Before the ball even came, he was already checking. Where are my players? Which team is which? Can I use the socks? Can I see the number? What if I pass to the wrong player again? What if everyone shouts? What if I get taken off? What if this is the moment they decide?

That is not football freedom. That is football played under a second game. The visible game was the match. The hidden game was colour doubt. Every player has pressure, but Number 12 carried an extra workload he could not name. He was not only reading football. He was checking whether the picture was safe enough to trust.

Over time, that hidden workload changed him. He started choosing safer passes. He took touches he did not always need. He hesitated when instinct should have carried him. He avoided some risks because guessing wrong was painful. He played smaller than the dream inside him, and because he played smaller, people judged the smaller version. That is how a player can disappear while still standing on the pitch. Not suddenly, but slowly: a late pass here, a wrong shirt there, a shout from the sideline, a substitute appearance, a quiet car journey, a coach’s doubt, a child’s private thought that maybe he is not what he hoped he was.

His mum watched from the side. She did what football parents do. She paid, travelled, waited, hoped and stood in weather that made no promises. She took him to matches when she was tired. She stood on muddy touchlines after long weeks. She gave weekends to the dream because that is what parents do when they believe in their child. Parents do not only pay with money. They pay with time, sleep, work days, family plans, petrol, hotels, food on the road, siblings waiting in cars, and quiet worry after bad games.

Years later, Number 12 understood more. He learned that his vision had not been the same as everyone else’s. He learned that colour could explain some of the confusion. He learned that eyesight mattered too. He learned that contact lenses helped him in ways glasses and sports goggles had not. Glasses could cause headaches or fall off. Sports goggles could protect, but they could also block parts of the side vision he needed in football. Contact lenses became practical because football needed movement, scanning, balance and peripheral awareness. The point was not that one solution works for everyone. The point was that eyesight and colour vision should have been checked properly, separately and earlier.

By the time he understood, he was older. The dream had changed shape. The years had moved. The chances had passed. The coaches who judged him had already judged him. The teams had moved on. The trials had moved on. The boy in the garden had become a man looking backwards, and looking backwards can hurt.

Colour blindness alone does not decide a football career. Poor eyesight alone does not decide a football career. Many things shape a player’s path: talent, coaching, injuries, confidence, money, timing, selection, family support, opportunity, luck and the brutal difficulty of becoming professional. This book will not pretend otherwise. But late discovery leaves a question that does not go away easily. What if I had known earlier?

What if he had known at eight? What if he had known at ten? What if he had known before the labels, before the bench, before the shame, before the safe passes, before the quiet car journeys, before he started believing the wrong story about himself? What if someone had asked, “What did you see?” What if a coach had changed the bibs? What if a parent had asked for colour vision testing? What if a club had checked kit contrast? What if teammates had learned to call names earlier? What if he had learned to use numbers, socks, shorts, positions, voices and movement patterns before doubt became habit?

Would he have become professional? Nobody can honestly say. But would he have had a clearer chance? Would he have understood himself sooner? Would he have carried less shame? Would he have played with more trust? Would his parent have known what to ask? Would his coach have judged the football more fairly? Those questions matter because Number 12 may not get those years back, but the next child can.

The next child can be tested earlier. The next child can be believed earlier. The next child can learn better cues earlier. The next child can have a coach who changes the setup without making a public scene. The next child can have a parent who asks the right question before confidence breaks. The next child can know that colour blindness is not stupidity, laziness, weak mentality or low football intelligence. It is not the end of a dream. But if nobody notices it, it can sit inside the dream like a hidden weight.

Number 12 did not need easier football. He needed clearer football. He needed to know what he was fighting. He needed adults to check the picture before they judged the player. That is where this book begins. Not with blame, not with pity, and not with excuses. It begins with the question nobody asked soon enough.

What did you see?

The Question Nobody Asked

The most important question in this book is not complicated. It does not require a medical degree, a coaching badge or expensive equipment. It does not accuse the player, embarrass the child or lower the standard. It simply slows the judgement down long enough to look at the moment properly.

What did you see?

That question changes the whole shape of the mistake. Without it, adults usually begin at the outcome. The pass went wrong. The player hesitated. The cone was missed. The drill broke down. The player looked nervous. The coach saw the error and corrected what the error appeared to be. But football decisions do not begin at the outcome. They begin with information. A player receives a picture, sorts the picture, trusts or doubts the picture, then acts. If that picture is unclear, the action may already be damaged before the technique, decision or confidence is judged.

“What did you see?” does not mean the player is excused from learning. It does not mean every answer will reveal a hidden vision issue. Sometimes the player will say, “I saw it and made the wrong pass.” That is football. Coach the pass. Sometimes the player will say, “I panicked.” Coach the pressure. Sometimes the player will say, “I did not scan.” Coach the scanning. But sometimes the answer will be different. Sometimes the player will say, “I thought he was on my team.” Sometimes they will say, “The bibs looked the same when everyone moved.” Sometimes they will say, “I could not see the cone quickly.” Sometimes they will say, “I was not sure, so I checked again.”

That is information a coach can use. It is information a parent can notice. It is information a player can build from. It turns a vague label into something practical. Slow becomes “needed a clearer cue.” Nervous becomes “worried about passing to the wrong shirt.” Not listening becomes “could not find the cone.” Poor awareness becomes “the picture was crowded and colour did not separate quickly enough.”

The question also protects dignity. A child who is asked what they saw is being invited into the truth of the moment. A child who is shouted at is often pushed deeper into silence. Many players, especially young ones, will not volunteer that colours are confusing if they think it will sound like an excuse. They may not even know colour is the issue. They only know some moments feel harder than they look. Asking the question gives them permission to describe the game from the inside.

That inside view matters because football is often judged from the outside. From the sideline, the pass looked simple. From the stand, the cone looked obvious. From the coach’s position, the bibs looked different. From the parent’s view, the run looked clear. But the player did not make the decision from the sideline, the stand, the coach’s position or the parent’s view. The player made it from inside speed, pressure, movement, bodies, shadows, noise and fear of being wrong.

The question does not weaken football standards. It sharpens them. A coach cannot fix the right problem if they name the wrong one. A parent cannot support the right issue if they keep searching in the wrong place. A player cannot build better habits if they think their whole self is the problem. The point is not to stop correcting mistakes. The point is to correct the correct mistake.

If the problem is technique, coach technique. If the problem is scanning, coach scanning. If the problem is communication, coach communication. If the problem is confidence, protect and rebuild confidence. But if the problem is that the visual cue was unclear, then change the cue. Use names. Use numbers. Use positions. Change the bibs. Move the cone. Add a shape. Check the kits. Give the player a reliable anchor before asking them to play faster.

“What did you see?” is not a soft question. It is a serious football question. It asks what information the player had at the moment of decision. It asks whether the game was testing football or testing colour. It asks whether the environment helped the player show ability or hid ability behind avoidable confusion.

A wrong pass may still be a wrong pass. A missed cone may still be a missed cone. A poor decision may still be a poor decision. But before the mistake becomes a label, ask what picture the player was playing inside. The answer may change everything that happens next.

Football Is Full of Colour Tests Nobody Calls Colour Tests

Nobody usually tells a child they are about to take a colour test at football. They call it training. They call it a match. They call it a drill, a warm-up, a tactical board, a rondo, a passing pattern, a match-day instruction or a coach’s shout across the pitch. But for some players, colour is the hidden test inside all of it.

A coach says, “Red team press.” A child has to know which team is red, separate the shirts quickly and act before the press is late. A coach says, “Dribble to the orange cone.” A child has to find orange against grass, mud, shadow or 3G surface while moving with the ball. A coach says, “Blue group rotate.” A child has to identify the group, remember the instruction and move before the drill collapses. To most players, these are simple instructions. To a colour-blind player, they may be another small moment of checking.

The problem is not that colour should never be used. Colour is useful. It helps football organise itself quickly. Kits separate teams. Bibs divide groups. Cones mark spaces. Lines define areas. Tactical boards explain shape. Screens and apps simplify information. The problem begins when colour becomes the only route to understanding something important. If the player misses the colour, the instruction disappears.

Football is full of these moments. Kits may clash in ways that are not obvious to people with typical colour vision. Two shirts can look different in the changing room and still become difficult in motion. Shorts and socks can matter as much as shirts, especially when players are seen from the side, from behind or through peripheral vision. Mud can reduce contrast. Low sun can flatten colours. Floodlights can change how kits appear. Shadows can make one team look more like another. A goalkeeper shirt can blend with outfield players. A referee shirt can add another moving colour into the picture.

Training can be just as difficult. Bib colours may be chosen because they are available, not because they separate well. Cones may be placed on grass without anyone asking whether they stand out for every player. A red or orange cone might look bright to a coach and still become unreliable for a colour-blind child at speed. A tactical board may use coloured magnets without numbers, initials, names or positions. A coach may explain a drill with colour words because colour is quick, not realising that quick for one player can be slow for another.

Even the modern parts of football can carry the same problem. Apps, analysis screens, substitution graphics, scoreboards, training diagrams and social media coaching images often rely on colour as if everyone receives it the same way. Red means one thing, green means another. Blue arrows show one movement, purple arrows show another. The information looks clean until colour is not a reliable separator.

For a colour-blind player, the issue is not always that they cannot see the colours at all. The issue is often that the colours do not separate quickly enough under football conditions. That difference is important. A child may correctly name a bib while standing still and still struggle to use that bib as a fast decision cue during a crowded drill. A player may know their team is wearing red and still hesitate when red, orange, brown, green, mud and shade begin to interfere with the picture.

That is why football must stop treating colour as decoration. Colour is information. If that information is unclear, the player’s football may be judged before the game has given them a fair picture. The answer is not to remove challenge. The answer is to remove avoidable confusion. Football can still be fast, demanding and competitive while being clearer. In fact, clearer football is often better football for everyone.

A session that uses names, numbers, positions, directions and strong contrast does not only help colour-blind players. It helps tired players, younger players, distracted players, players under pressure, players who missed the first instruction and players who are still learning the game. Clear information speeds up training. It reduces wasted explanations. It makes mistakes easier to understand. It allows the coach to judge the football more accurately.

The hidden colour tests in football are not impossible to fix. Many of them are simple. Change the bibs. Add numbers. Use white or blue cones when they stand out better. Avoid relying on orange cones on grass as the only important cue. Check kits in greyscale. Look at shirts, shorts and socks together. Use names early. Call positions. Give directions. Mark zones with more than colour. Put numbers or initials on tactical magnets. Ask the player privately which situations are hardest.

The goal is not perfect football. Football will always be messy. The goal is not to protect players from pressure. Pressure is part of the game. The goal is to stop avoidable colour confusion pretending to be football difficulty. A child should be challenged by the opponent, the speed of play, the tactical problem and their own development. They should not be trapped by a cone they cannot see quickly, a bib they cannot trust or a kit clash nobody checked.

Football is full of colour tests nobody calls colour tests. Once you see them, you can start changing them.

What This Book Will Help You Do

This book is written for the people around the player as much as for the player themselves. Colour blindness in football is rarely solved by one person noticing one thing on one day. It is usually understood through patterns: what happens at home, what happens at school, what happens in training, what happens in matches, what happens under pressure, and what changes when the visual picture becomes clearer.

The Hidden Scale

Colour blindness is often treated as a small issue because people imagine they would notice it if it mattered. But hidden does not mean rare. Hidden often means nobody has been looking in the right way.

Many children with colour vision deficiency do not know they see differently. They grow up inside their own version of colour and assume everyone else receives the same picture. If adults have not been trained to notice the signs, the child’s difficulty may be explained as something else for years: carelessness, nerves, immaturity, poor concentration, lack of confidence or weak decision-making.

That is why scale matters. If colour blindness were extremely rare, football might treat it as an unusual individual issue. If it were always obvious, every player could speak up and explain it. They are estimates and rules of thumb, not exact counts of footballers. But even as estimates, they change the conversation.

On a football pitch, it may appear in kits, socks, shorts, goalkeeper shirts, referee shirts, bibs, cones, balls, lines, tactical boards, training apps and instructions.

The gap between dream and outcome is enormous. That does not mean children should stop dreaming. Dreams give children discipline, joy, identity, friendship, ambition and purpose. The point is not that colour blindness guarantees a lost dream. It does not. The point is that the pathway is already difficult enough. No child should be trying to climb it while carrying an avoidable visual barrier nobody has checked.

The hidden scale is not only about how many children may be colour blind. It is about how many moments could be made clearer before they become wounds.

Chapter 1 — The Mistake That Was Never Just a Mistake

The pass went straight to the wrong shirt.

It was the kind of mistake everyone sees at once. The ball left his foot, travelled across the pitch, and found an opponent so cleanly that for a second it looked like he had meant it. The other team took the gift and broke forward. His teammates threw their arms out. A parent on the sideline made that sharp disappointed sound people make when they cannot believe what they have just watched. The coach turned away before turning back again, already carrying the words in his face.

From the outside, the mistake was obvious. It looked like bad awareness. It looked like a player who had not scanned. It looked like a child who had panicked, switched off, or failed to do the simple thing. The pass was wrong, and because the pass was wrong, the explanation seemed easy.

But the real story did not begin when the ball reached the wrong player. It began half a second earlier, inside a picture nobody else had checked.

That is the part football often misses. The game is judged by outcomes, but decisions are made from information. A player does not pass from nowhere. He receives a picture first. He sees shirts, movement, pressure, grass, shadows, socks, space, body shape, the ball, the direction of play and the time available. He sorts that picture quickly, trusts it or doubts it, then acts. When the picture is clear, the decision can be judged more fairly. When the picture is unclear, the mistake may have started before the pass was even made.

In that moment, the player may not have been thinking, “How do I break the line?” He may have been thinking, “Which one is my teammate?” He may not have been thinking, “Can I play forward?” He may have been thinking, “Is that shirt safe?” He may not have been thinking like a lazy player, a careless player or a player without football intelligence. He may have been thinking like a player trying to survive uncertainty at speed.

The difference matters because football punishes the uncertainty before anyone understands it. A half-second of checking can close a passing lane. A moment of doubt can invite pressure. One extra touch can make the player look slow. One safe pass can make the player look scared. One wrong-team pass can become the story people tell about the child.

The mistake becomes a label.

Slow.

Nervous.

Careless.

Not switched on.

Not brave enough.

Not good enough.

Once a label forms, it becomes hard to remove. A child can make ten good decisions and still be remembered for the one mistake that confirmed what adults already suspected. That is dangerous, because the label may be describing the wrong problem. A player who hesitates because the shirt cue is unclear does not need the same help as a player who hesitates because he has not scanned. A player who misses a cone because it blends into the surface does not need the same correction as a player who ignored the instruction. A player who passes to the wrong team because the kits collapsed together in movement does not need to be told only to “wake up.” He needs the picture checked.

This does not mean the player is excused from learning. That is not the point. A colour-blind player still has to become a better footballer. They still need technique, awareness, courage, communication, scanning, fitness and decision-making. They still need to be coached. They still need to accept responsibility when the football action is poor. But responsibility becomes more useful when the problem has been named correctly.

A bad pass can be coached. A hidden visual problem has to be noticed first.

Many young players do not know how to explain the difference. They do not have the language. They do not say, “I am experiencing unreliable colour separation under match pressure.” They do not say, “The opponent’s shirt and my teammate’s shirt lost contrast in my peripheral vision when both players moved through shadow.” They do not say, “I needed to verify the sock cue because the shirt colour was not enough.” Children do not speak like that. Many adults do not speak like that either.

A child says nothing.

Or they say, “I don’t know.”

Or they look down.

That silence is easy to misread. Adults may think the player is sulking, avoiding responsibility or not listening. But sometimes the child really does not know what happened. They only know that the game became unclear, the decision felt crowded, the mistake happened quickly, and the explanation arrived before they could find their own.

That is how a hidden visual problem can become an emotional wound. The child is not only dealing with the mistake. They are dealing with being misunderstood in public. Football mistakes are rarely private. They happen in front of teammates, coaches, parents, opponents and sometimes strangers. A child learns very quickly which mistakes cause noise. They learn the passes that make teammates groan. They learn the moments that make the coach lose trust. They learn the look from the sideline before anyone says anything.

If colour confusion is part of the pattern, the child may begin protecting themselves without realising it. They take an extra touch because they want to be sure. They play backwards because backwards feels safer. They stop switching play because the risk of the wrong shirt feels too high. They avoid crowded areas. They stop demanding the ball. They choose the obvious pass, even when the better pass exists, because the obvious pass is easier to verify.

From the outside, this can look like a confidence problem. Sometimes it is. But confidence may be the result, not the cause. A player who has been embarrassed by unclear information learns to fear unclear information. They do not simply lack bravery. They may have learned that bravery without a reliable picture can hurt.

That is why one wrong pass can be more than one wrong pass. Not always. Sometimes it is just football. But when certain mistakes repeat in certain visual conditions, the pattern deserves attention. Does the player struggle more when bibs are similar? Do they hesitate more under floodlights? Do they make more wrong-team passes when kits are dark against dark, red against green, orange against grass, or when socks and shorts get muddy? Do they look better in clear drills than in crowded matches? Do they ask who is on which team? Do they track numbers more than shirts? Do they seem relieved when teams are obvious? Do they become tense before receiving the ball in certain games?

These questions do not diagnose colour blindness. They do something more basic and more useful at the start: they stop adults from rushing to the wrong explanation.

A parent may see this before anyone else. Parents often notice the emotional pattern before they understand the visual pattern. They see the child who was excited before the game become quiet afterwards. They see the frustration after being shouted at. They see the strange difference between one match and another. They may know their child can play. They may have watched them practise for years. They may have paid for training, boots, travel, trials and tournaments. They may have given up weekends and work days because the dream mattered. Then they stand on the sideline and watch the child look lost in moments that should suit them.

The parent starts searching for explanations. Maybe it is confidence. Maybe the level is too high. Maybe the coach does not believe in them. Maybe the child needs more practice. Maybe they are tired. Maybe they are not brave enough. Maybe they are not as good as everyone hoped. Those possibilities may all be part of the story, but if nobody thinks to ask what the child saw, the family may spend years trying to solve the wrong problem.

That is where regret can begin later. Regret does not always arrive during the journey. Often, during the journey, everyone is too busy trying to keep going. The child trains. The parent drives. The coach selects. The games come and go. The dream survives on small moments: one good performance, one compliment, one trial, one goal, one coach who seems interested, one day where the picture is clear enough and the player feels like himself.

Only later, when the truth appears, does the past reorganise itself. The player looks back and sees patterns that were invisible at the time. The parent remembers questions they did not know to ask. The wrong socks, the colour confusion at home, the discomfort in certain kits, the hesitation in certain matches, the way the child seemed to worry before receiving the ball. Suddenly, old moments carry new meaning.

That new meaning can bring relief. It can also bring grief. Relief because the struggle has a name. Grief because the name arrived late.

The player may think, “What if I had known earlier?” The parent may think the same. What if they had asked for a colour vision test when the child was younger? What if the coach had used numbers and names sooner? What if the player had learned to use socks, shorts, voices, positions and movement patterns before the fear of the wrong shirt became a habit? What if the child had spent less time thinking they were slow and more time learning how to read the game in a way that worked for them?

Nobody can honestly rewrite a football career with one answer. That would be unfair and untrue. Colour blindness does not decide everything. Football is too complex for that. Talent, timing, injury, coaching, money, growth, competition, opportunity and luck all matter. But it is also wrong to dismiss the question completely. If a player was judged for years inside unclear information, then something important may have been missed. The issue may not explain the whole story, but it may explain enough to matter.

Coaches are part of this story too, and not as villains. Most coaches are trying to help. They want players to improve. They want quicker decisions, sharper scanning, stronger communication and braver football. They correct what they think they see. The difficulty is that a coach can only coach the problem they have identified. If the coach thinks the player is lazy, the response will be different from if the coach thinks the player is unsure. If the coach thinks the player lacks bravery, the response will be different from if the coach realises the player has learned to avoid visual risk. If the coach thinks the child is not listening, the response will be different from if the cone cannot be found quickly enough.

A good coach does not need to become an eye specialist. But a good coach can become more curious about information. Instead of beginning with, “Why did you do that?” the coach can ask, “What did you see?” Instead of assuming, “He has to move it quicker,” the coach can ask, “What cue did he have?” Instead of shouting, “You should have seen that,” the coach can ask, “Was the picture clear?”

That does not make the game soft. It makes the coaching sharper. Better questions produce better answers. Better answers produce better interventions. If the player simply made a poor decision, coach the decision. If the player failed to scan, coach the scan. If the player used the wrong body shape, coach the body shape. If the player could not separate the shirts quickly, then coach the environment first: change the cue, improve the contrast, add numbers, use names, adjust bibs, check kits, make the picture clearer. Then judge the football.

The mistake that was never just a mistake is not always about colour blindness. It is about the danger of judging visible outcomes without checking hidden inputs. Football is full of inputs. Light is an input. Contrast is an input. Kit design is an input. Bib choice is an input. Cone colour is an input. Coaching language is an input. The player’s eyesight, colour vision, confidence and memory of past mistakes are inputs too.

When adults ignore those inputs, they may judge the child as if the child chose the confusion. They may demand speed while designing delay into the session. They may demand bravery while giving the player a picture they do not fully trust. They may demand confidence while repeatedly exposing the child to public mistakes that could have been reduced with clearer information.

A player should be challenged by football. They should be challenged by the opponent, the speed of play, the need to scan, the pressure of decisions and the responsibility of competing. They should not be trapped by avoidable confusion that nobody thought to check.

This is the first shift the book asks for. Before the label, look at the pattern. Before the judgement, look at the picture. Before deciding what the player lacks, ask what information the player had.

The wrong pass still matters. The child may still need coaching. But the answer may not be to shout louder, criticise harder or demand courage from a player who is already fighting a hidden uncertainty. The answer may be to make the game visible enough for the player’s real football to be seen.

That is where fairness begins. Not by lowering the standard. By making sure the standard is measuring football.

Chapter 2 — The Colours They Could Not Trust

Most colour-blind players do not live in a black-and-white world.

That is one of the first myths that needs to disappear, because the myth hides the real problem. When people hear the words colour blind, they often imagine a player seeing everything in grey. They imagine a world with no colour at all. So when a child correctly says the grass is green, or knows their kit is red, or names the blue cone during training, adults assume colour blindness cannot be part of the issue.

That assumption can be wrong.

Many colour-blind people see colour. The difficulty is often not that colour has vanished. The difficulty is that some colours do not separate reliably. They may look closer together than they do for other people. They may lose strength in certain light. They may blend into backgrounds, collapse into similar shades, or become harder to trust when speed, distance, mud, shadows and movement enter the picture.

In everyday life, that can be inconvenient. In football, it can become performance-changing.

Football does not ask a player to sit still and name colours calmly. It asks them to use colour while running, scanning, turning, defending, passing, pressing, listening to instructions and trying not to lose the ball. A player may be able to identify a colour with time, but still struggle to use that colour quickly enough when the game is moving. That difference is one of the most important ideas in this book.

The problem is not always colour naming.

The problem is fast colour separation.

A child may know that a cone is orange because they have learned what orange usually looks like. They may still struggle to find that cone quickly on grass. A player may know their team is wearing red. They may still hesitate if the opposition kit, goalkeeper shirt, referee shirt, shadow, mud, socks or shorts make the picture uncertain. A player may pass a normal eyesight chart and still have a colour vision problem if colour vision was not specifically tested.

Sharp eyesight and reliable colour separation are not the same thing.

That point matters because many families think an eye test has already ruled out vision as part of the problem. A child may have been told they can see clearly. They may read letters on a chart, recognise faces, watch television, use a phone and play football in the garden. But colour vision testing is a specific question. If nobody asks that question, the answer may stay hidden.

For Number 12, that difference came too late. He did not grow up thinking, “I am colour blind.” He thought he saw the same world everyone else saw. He could name colours. He could play football. He could train well when the picture was clear. So when matches became confusing, he did not blame vision. He blamed himself. That is what many children do. They do not have a second version of the world to compare against. Their normal is the only normal they know.

This is why colour blindness can stay hidden for years. Children learn colour names from memory, correction and context. They know the sky is called blue, grass is called green, a stop sign is red, their school jumper has a certain colour, and their team kit has a name adults use. But knowing the word is not the same as seeing the separation clearly. A child may learn the label while still struggling with the contrast.

Football makes that harder because it rarely gives children time to reason through colour. The game demands recognition before language. The player does not think, “That is a red shirt, therefore that is my teammate.” The player sees a moving shape, receives a cue, trusts it and acts. If the cue is weak, the trust becomes slower. If the trust is slower, the football becomes slower.

That is how a colour problem can look like a football problem.

A late pass may look like poor decision-making. It may actually begin with delayed recognition. A player holding the ball too long may look like they are scared. They may be checking whether the forward option is safe. A child missing a cone may look like they did not listen. They may be searching for a colour that does not stand out from the surface quickly enough. A player choosing the safe pass may look like they lack bravery. They may have learned that uncertain colour cues can lead to public embarrassment.

The most common inherited colour vision difficulties involve red-green colour vision. That does not mean only red and green matter. In football, the problem can spread into many combinations because colours do not appear in perfect laboratory conditions. Red, green, orange, brown, grey and certain dark shades can become difficult. Blue and purple can be confused for some players. Bright colours can still fail if they sit on the wrong background or appear under poor light. A cone that looks clear in a bag may look different on wet grass. A bib that looks bright in a changing room may look less useful when players are running through shade.

Protan-type difficulties can make reds appear darker or less noticeable. Deutan-type difficulties can affect green-related separation. Tritan-type difficulties, involving blue-yellow perception, are much rarer but can still matter. Complete black-and-white vision is rare, and it is not what most people mean when they talk about common colour blindness. The practical football point is not to turn parents and coaches into eye specialists. The practical point is to understand that different players may struggle with different colour combinations, and the only safe approach is to avoid relying on colour alone when the information matters.

That means football should be careful with common risky combinations. Red against green. Red against orange. Green against yellow. Blue against purple. Dark against dark. Orange or red cones on grass. Low-contrast lines on a pitch. Goalkeeper shirts that sit too close to outfield kits. Referee shirts that add another confusing colour into the game. Bibs that are different in name but similar in tone. Kits that look separate before kick-off but merge once mud, sweat, shadow and speed change the picture.

The word tone matters here. Sometimes two colours look different to a person with typical colour vision but become similar in lightness or darkness. When that happens, a greyscale phone photo can be useful. If two kits look similar in greyscale, there may be a contrast problem. It is not a perfect scientific test, and it does not replace proper guidance, but it is a simple practical habit that can reveal clashes people might otherwise miss.

The important thing is to look at the whole kit, not just the shirt. Football is not always viewed from the front. Players see each other from the side, from behind and through peripheral vision. Shirts fold. Bodies turn. Mud covers fabric. The ball arrives while the player is moving. Socks and shorts can become vital cues. A team with similar shirts but very different socks may be easier to separate than a team where the entire outline becomes too close. A player who cannot trust shirts may learn to read socks, shorts, numbers, body shape, voice or position instead.

Those alternative cues are not signs of weakness. They are intelligent adaptations. A player who learns to use numbers, voices, movement, positions and direction of attack is not asking for an easier game. They are building a more reliable information system. Good footballers already use more than one cue. They read body shape, pressure, space, timing and communication. Colour-blind players may simply need to make that multi-cue system more deliberate.

The trouble comes when the environment forces colour to carry too much of the meaning. If the coach says “go to red,” and red is the only cue, the player has no backup. If the tactical board uses only coloured magnets, the player has no second route to understanding. If the drill depends on orange cones against grass, the player must solve colour before solving football. If the team relies only on shirt colour when shirts are unclear, the player must check when others can act.

That extra checking may look small. It is not small to the player. It costs time and energy. It can break rhythm. It can make the player feel behind the game. It can create a strange kind of self-consciousness, where the player is not only asking, “What is the best football decision?” but also, “Can I trust what I think I saw?” That second question can become heavy. It is one reason worry becomes such a major part of this book.

For a young player, this can be emotionally confusing. They may feel they can play. They may have proof. They may be good in the garden, good in one-to-one coaching, good in clear drills, good in small groups, good in matches where the kits are obvious. Then another environment appears and their confidence drops. Adults may call that inconsistency. The child may call it failure. But sometimes the difference is not the child’s desire or ability. Sometimes the difference is the reliability of the picture.

This is also why girls must not be left out of the conversation. Colour blindness is less common in girls, but less common does not mean impossible. When a girl is affected, the football problem is just as real. A confusing bib is still confusing. A cone that disappears into grass still disappears. A kit clash still slows recognition. A player who hesitates can still be misread. The statistic may be smaller, but the dignity owed to the player is not smaller.

For parents, the lesson is to look for patterns without jumping to conclusions. Does the child confuse certain colours at home? Do they mix up clothing, socks or school materials? Do they avoid colour-based tasks? Do they become frustrated when corrected about colours? In football, do they struggle more with certain bibs, cones, kits, light or surfaces? Do they ask who is on which team? Do they seem more confident when the teams are visually clear? These signs do not prove colour blindness. They are clues worth checking.

For coaches, the lesson is even more practical. Do not make colour the only instruction. Use colour plus another cue. Say “red team, attacking the clubhouse,” or “blue group, number two,” or “the cone nearest the goal,” or “the left-hand gate,” or “Jamie’s team.” Add names, numbers, directions, shapes and positions. Use stronger contrast when choosing bibs. Think about cones against the surface. Check kits before judging a wrong-team pass as only poor awareness. Ask privately if certain colours are difficult. Make the setup clearer for everyone.

For players, the lesson is personal. If colours have confused you, it does not mean you are stupid. If you need to use numbers or socks, it does not mean you are less of a footballer. If you have been worrying about passing to the wrong shirt, that worry makes sense. The goal is not to become someone else. The goal is to learn the cues that work for you, explain them when needed, and build confidence around a picture you can trust.

Colour blindness is not the absence of football intelligence. In many cases, it is the reason a player may need to use football intelligence more deliberately. They may need to recognise patterns, listen earlier, scan differently, communicate better and build stronger anchors. That can become part of their game. But it is much easier if they know early enough.

Late discovery hurts because it turns old moments into unanswered questions. The player may look back and wonder why they were called slow when they were checking, why they were called nervous when they were uncertain, why they were called careless when the colours were not clear. The parent may wonder why nobody suggested colour vision testing sooner. The coach may remember a player who looked talented in some situations and lost in others.

The purpose of this chapter is to make that future regret less likely. A child should not have to wait until the dream is damaged before understanding that colours may have been part of the struggle. A parent should not have to guess for years. A coach should not have to misread the same pattern over and over.

The colours they could not trust may have been telling a story. Football has to learn how to read it.

Chapter 3 — The Hidden Millions

Hidden does not mean rare. Sometimes hidden simply means nobody has been looking in the right way.

Colour blindness is often treated as a small issue because people imagine it would be obvious if it mattered. They imagine a child constantly naming colours incorrectly, choosing wildly mismatched clothes, or announcing that the world looks strange. They imagine something easy to spot. But for many children, colour vision deficiency is not obvious in that way. It can sit quietly inside ordinary life, disguised by memory, habit, guessing, context and silence.

A child learns that grass is called green because adults call it green. They learn that the sky is blue, the school jumper is navy, the stop sign is red and their team shirt has a colour name. They may repeat those names correctly for years. That does not mean the colours separate for them in the same way they separate for everyone else. It may only mean they have learned the language of colour well enough to move through the world without being noticed.

Football makes this hidden issue harder to ignore, because football uses colour quickly. It does not use colour only as decoration. It uses colour as information. The colour of a shirt tells a player whether the pass is safe. The colour of a bib tells them which team is pressing. The colour of a cone tells them where to run, turn, stop or defend. The colour of a goalkeeper shirt, referee shirt, tactical board magnet or training app graphic may affect how the player reads the moment.

When colour is everywhere in a sport, colour vision is not a side issue. It becomes part of how the game is understood. And when a common issue is hidden inside an information system that nobody thinks to question, children can be misread for years.

The scale is larger than many people expect. A widely used UK rule of thumb is that around one in twelve males and around one in two hundred females have colour vision deficiency. More recent paediatric research has estimated colour vision deficiency among children and adolescents at around 2.59% overall, with higher prevalence in males than females. Applied carefully to global child-population estimates, that suggests tens of millions of children worldwide may have congenital colour vision deficiency.

Those figures must be handled honestly. They are not exact counts of footballers. They do not tell us precisely how many children in the world are playing football with colour vision deficiency. They do not prove how many players have been misjudged, how many have lost confidence or how many have missed opportunities. They are scale estimates and prevalence figures, not a complete football census.

But even as estimates, they change the conversation.

This is not one child somewhere. This is not one unlucky player in one team. This is not a rare excuse invented after a bad game. This is a hidden access issue that may already be present in classrooms, school PE lessons, grassroots clubs, academies, tournaments, trials and match days.

A typical school may already have affected pupils. A typical male football squad may statistically include a colour-blind player. A tournament with hundreds of children may include many players who process colour differently, whether they know it or not. Some will have been tested. Some will not. Some will have parents who know. Some will have parents who have never considered it. Some will have coaches who understand. Many will not.

That is why the hidden scale matters. It changes the responsibility from special reaction to normal good practice. If colour blindness may already be present, then clubs and coaches should not wait for a child to announce it before making important information clearer. Many children will not announce it because they do not know. Some will not announce it because they are embarrassed. Some will not announce it because they are afraid of being treated differently. Some will have learned that staying quiet feels safer than explaining something adults may dismiss.

The problem is not only that colour blindness exists. The problem is that football often behaves as if it does not.

Football assumes that every player can use shirt colour quickly. It assumes that every player can separate bibs at speed. It assumes that every player can see the orange cone on grass, the red line on a surface, the coloured zones on a tactical board or the difference between two kits under poor light. It assumes that if the coach can see it, the child can see it. It assumes that if most players understand the drill, the visual design must be clear enough.

Those assumptions can be wrong.

The child who struggles may not look like a child with a vision issue. They may look like a child who lacks focus. They may look like a player who switches off in certain drills. They may look like someone who has poor awareness in matches but looks fine in training. They may look like a player who is strangely inconsistent. They may look like a child who plays with confidence when the teams are clearly different, then becomes cautious when colours are closer together.

Because the issue is hidden, adults often search for explanations they already understand. They talk about confidence, nerves, concentration, maturity, effort, decision-making or attitude. Those things may matter. They often do. But if colour vision is never considered, one part of the story may be missed completely.

That matters for players because labels stick. A child who is repeatedly called slow may begin to play slowly. A child who is repeatedly told they panic may begin to expect panic. A child who is repeatedly corrected for mistakes they cannot explain may stop believing their own ability. The hidden scale of colour blindness is not only a statistical issue. It is a confidence issue. It is a development issue. It is a fairness issue.

It matters for parents because parents often carry the confusion home. A parent may see their child look talented in one environment and lost in another. They may watch the child practise beautifully in the garden, perform well in clear drills, then struggle in certain match conditions. They may wonder whether it is pressure, coaching, confidence, tiredness or the level of football. They may spend money and time trying to solve the wrong problem because nobody has explained that colour vision might be worth checking.

For some families, the investment is enormous. Football can become the centre of weekends, evenings and family planning. Parents pay for subs, boots, kit, private coaching, tournaments, trials, camps, travel, hotels and food on the road. They miss work. They lose wages. They take siblings to cold touchlines. They organise education and family routines around training and matches. They do this because the dream matters to the child, and because parents often hope that effort, sacrifice and opportunity will give their child the best chance.

That is why late discovery can be painful. It is not simply that money was spent. It is that hope was spent. Time was spent. Confidence was spent. Years were spent trying to understand a problem that might have had a name earlier. The parent may look back and wonder whether they missed signs. The player may look back and wonder whether they could have adapted sooner. Those questions are difficult, and this book must handle them carefully. Colour blindness alone does not decide a football career. But unrecognised colour blindness can add avoidable confusion to moments where confidence, selection, money, opportunity and results are on the line.

It matters for coaches too. The hidden scale means coaches may already have colour-blind players in their sessions without knowing. That does not make coaches guilty. Many coaches have never been taught to think about colour vision. They may be volunteers giving up evenings and weekends. They may be trying to manage large groups, limited equipment, difficult weather, parent expectations, match pressure and club demands. They correct the problems they can see.

But if the visual setup is unclear, a good coach can still end up correcting the wrong problem. The player who looks slow may be checking the picture. The player who looks nervous may be protecting themselves from another wrong-shirt pass. The player who misses the cone may not have ignored the instruction. The cone may have disappeared into the surface. The team that keeps misunderstanding a drill may not lack attention. The drill may rely too heavily on colour.

The hidden scale should make coaches more curious, not more defensive. It should encourage them to build clarity into normal coaching practice. Stronger bib contrast, clearer cones, non-colour cues, names, numbers, positions, directions, shapes and greyscale kit checks are not special treatment. They are good design. They help everyone, including players who are tired, younger, distracted, new to the language of football, under pressure or simply trying to process instructions quickly.

It matters for clubs and schools because children should not have to depend on luck. A player should not only receive clear support if they happen to meet the one coach who understands colour blindness. A pupil should not only get better PE instructions if one teacher has personal experience of the issue. A trial should not only be fair if someone remembers to check kit contrast. Systems matter because children move through systems. Teams change. Coaches change. Kits change. Schools change. Age groups change. The child remains the one carrying the consequences.

A colour-safe approach does not need to be complicated. It starts with a simple assumption: someone in the group may not receive colour information in the same way as everyone else. From there, the practical steps are straightforward. Do not rely on colour alone. Use contrast. Add labels. Use numbers. Use shapes. Use directions. Use names. Check the environment. Ask better questions. Protect privacy. Make clarity normal.

This approach is especially important because many children do not want to stand out. Even if they know they are colour blind, they may not want the whole team to know. They may worry that teammates will laugh, coaches will doubt them or parents will become overprotective. A child may prefer quiet support. Universal clarity allows adults to improve the environment without turning one player into the public reason for every change.

Instead of saying, “We are changing this because one player cannot see colours,” a coach can say, “We are using names and numbers because good teams communicate clearly.” Instead of announcing a child’s difficulty, a club can buy better bibs. Instead of making a pupil explain themselves, a school can avoid colour-only instructions as standard practice. The player receives help without being exposed.

The hidden scale also challenges the idea that colour blindness only matters at elite level. Professional kit clashes on television may attract attention because fans complain, broadcasters notice and governing bodies respond. But the grassroots player on a muddy pitch may face the same practical issue without the same protection. There may be no spare kit. No official checking contrast. No awareness from the adults. No camera showing the problem. No one asking whether the player could actually separate the teams at speed.

In some ways, the child at grassroots level may be more vulnerable. They may not know they are colour blind. Their coach may be a volunteer. Their parent may not know what signs to watch for. Their club may have limited equipment. Their school may rely on old cones and bibs. The child may be judged quickly because grassroots football can still be emotionally intense, especially when dreams, trials, selection and parental sacrifice are involved.

The hidden millions are not all future professionals. Most children who play football will not become professional, with or without colour blindness. But that does not make the issue smaller. Football is not only about becoming professional. It is also about confidence, belonging, movement, friendships, discipline, joy, identity and self-belief. A child who is repeatedly misunderstood may lose more than a place in a team. They may lose trust in themselves.

That is why the scale of this issue should matter to everyone in football. Not because every colour-blind child will struggle. Not because every colour-blind player needs the same support. Not because colour blindness should become an excuse for poor play. It should matter because the cost of improving clarity is often low, while the cost of missing the issue can be high.

A clearer cone is not expensive compared with years of confusion. A second cue is not difficult compared with a child losing confidence. A private question is not a burden compared with a player believing they are stupid. A greyscale kit check takes seconds compared with a match being shaped by avoidable confusion. A parent asking for colour vision testing is a small step compared with years of not knowing.

The hidden scale should not create panic. It should create responsibility. It should make football more careful with colour, more curious about patterns and more respectful of the fact that not every player receives the same picture. Once that truth is accepted, the solutions become practical.

If colour is important, add another cue. If a kit might clash, check it. If a cone matters, make sure it stands out. If a player hesitates repeatedly in certain visual conditions, ask what they saw. If a child discovers colour blindness late, treat the grief with care, but use the lesson to help the next child earlier.

The hidden millions do not need pity. They need football to stop assuming the picture is the same for everyone.

Chapter 4 — Why Nobody Saw It Sooner

A child cannot explain a different picture if they have never seen through anyone else’s eyes.

That is one of the reasons colour blindness can stay hidden for so long. Children usually believe their version of the world is the normal one. If a colour looks dull, weak, similar or unreliable to them, they may assume it looks that way to everyone. If a cone is hard to pick out from the grass, they may assume the drill is just hard. If two kits seem close at speed, they may assume they are slow to react. If a coloured worksheet, tactical board or training instruction takes longer to understand, they may assume they are the problem.

Most children do not walk into football and announce, “I process some colour information differently.” They do not have the words, the comparison or the confidence. They only have the experience. Some parts of the game feel clear. Other parts feel crowded, uncertain or strangely difficult. The adults around them may see the struggle, but not the reason for it.

This is how colour blindness gets missed. Not because nobody cares, but because everyone is often looking at the wrong thing.

Parents may look at behaviour. Coaches may look at performance. Teachers may look at attention. Teammates may look at the mistake. The child may look inward and blame themselves. Each person sees a piece of the puzzle, but the full picture remains hidden because nobody has yet asked whether the child is receiving the same visual information as everyone else.

At home, the signs can be small. A child may mix up socks, choose unusual clothing combinations, struggle with certain coloured pens, misread colour-coded homework, avoid colouring tasks, ask for help with maps or charts, or become annoyed when corrected about colour. They may learn to use clues that adults do not notice: position, labels, memory, brightness, shape, smell, texture or context. They may get through everyday life by guessing well enough that nobody thinks to ask deeper questions.

At school, the signs can also be misread. A child who struggles with a colour-coded diagram may look careless. A pupil who takes longer with a map, graph or science chart may look slow. A child who copies another pupil’s colour choices may look unoriginal or distracted. A child who avoids tasks where colour mistakes are visible may be seen as lacking confidence. The problem can sit quietly inside ordinary schoolwork, especially if the child is intelligent enough to compensate.

In football, the signs are often louder but still misunderstood. A wrong-team pass looks like poor awareness. A missed cone looks like poor listening. A late pass looks like slow decision-making. A safe pass looks like lack of bravery. A player who hesitates looks nervous. A player who asks who is on which team looks inattentive. A player who performs well in one match and struggles in another looks inconsistent.

The word inconsistent can hide many sins. It is one of the easiest labels in football because it sounds like an explanation, but often it is only a description. A child plays well in one environment and poorly in another. The adult says, “He is inconsistent.” But the better question is: what changed? Did the kit colours change? Did the bibs change? Did the light change? Did the pitch surface change? Did the drill rely more heavily on coloured cones? Did mud reduce contrast? Did shadows make the teams harder to separate? Did the player move from a clear training setup into a crowded match?

Without those questions, the player carries the label and the environment escapes examination.

Number 12 lived inside that misunderstanding. In some training sessions, he looked like he could play. In some matches, especially when the picture was clearer, he felt closer to the player he wanted to be. But in other games, the uncertainty came back. Shirts seemed to move into each other. Socks and shorts became more important than anyone realised. The ball arrived before he fully trusted the picture. He checked, delayed, played safe or chose wrong. From the side, it looked like nerves. From inside, it felt like trying to make a fast decision with information that would not settle.

Nobody saw it sooner because the explanation everyone already had was easier. He needed to be quicker. He needed more confidence. He needed to stop panicking. He needed to concentrate. He needed to become braver. Those explanations were familiar. Colour blindness was not.

That is another reason the issue gets missed: adults often explain behaviour using the categories they already know. In football, those categories are usually technical, tactical, physical or psychological. If a player makes a mistake, people look for a football reason. Poor first touch. Bad scan. Weak body shape. Slow decision. Lack of confidence. Fear of pressure. Poor attitude. Low intensity. Those explanations may sometimes be correct, but they are not the only possibilities.

Vision sits underneath all of them. A player cannot scan effectively if the information they scan is unclear. A player cannot decide quickly if identification takes longer. A player cannot play bravely if previous visual uncertainty has taught them that risk brings embarrassment. A player cannot communicate their needs if they do not know the source of the problem.

This does not mean coaches should stop coaching football. It means they should understand that football performance begins with perception. The player sees, sorts, trusts and acts. If the first step is compromised, the later steps may look worse than they really are.

Parents can miss it for a different reason. They see their child in many settings, but they may not know which details matter. They may notice the wrong socks but think it is carelessness. They may notice the child hates certain homework but think it is boredom. They may notice football hesitation but think it is nerves. They may see the child name colours correctly and assume colour blindness is impossible. They may trust that a routine eyesight check covered everything, not realising that colour vision testing may need to be asked about specifically.

Parents also want to be fair. Many do not want to make excuses for their child. They may worry that raising colour blindness with a coach will sound like they are protecting the child from criticism. They may delay asking because they do not want to be “that parent.” Meanwhile, the child continues to carry the pattern.

Coaches can miss it because training environments are busy. A grassroots coach may have fifteen children, limited time, old bibs, mixed ability levels, parents watching, another team waiting for the pitch and a session plan to get through. They may not have been taught about colour vision. They may have no reason to suspect it. If most players follow the drill, the setup seems fine. If one player struggles, it is natural to focus on the player.

But the environment can single out a child without anybody meaning harm. A cone colour, bib choice or kit combination may create a barrier for one player while appearing normal to everyone else. The coach sees one child struggling and assumes the child is the variable. Sometimes the variable is the design.

Schools can miss it because colour is everywhere in learning. Charts, graphs, maps, diagrams, reading systems, behaviour charts, sports teams and classroom labels often use colour to organise information. If colour is supported by text, shape, position or labels, many children cope. If colour is the only cue, some children struggle. But unless teachers are trained to think about colour vision, the struggle may be absorbed into broader ideas about attention, confidence or academic ability.

The child may also become skilled at hiding. Children often hide what embarrasses them. They copy the person next to them. They wait to see where others run. They choose the same colour another child picked. They avoid volunteering. They laugh off mistakes. They say they forgot. They say they were not listening. They accept blame because the truth is harder to explain.

In football, hiding can look like safe play. A player who is unsure may avoid the pass that requires instant shirt recognition. They may wait for a voice. They may pass backwards. They may choose the player closest to them. They may avoid receiving on the half-turn. They may drift away from crowded areas. They may look like they lack ambition, when in truth they are trying not to be exposed.

This hiding costs energy. The player is not only playing the game. They are managing the fear of being wrong in a way nobody else can see. That fear can become part of their football identity. They begin to expect uncertainty. They begin to prepare for criticism. They begin to play with the memory of previous mistakes. Worry becomes another opponent.

This is why late discovery can be so painful. When a player finally learns they are colour blind, the past does not stay still. Old moments return with new meaning. The player remembers the wrong pass, the missed cone, the shout, the bench, the trial, the safe pass, the moment they stopped asking for the ball. The parent remembers the money, the travel, the weekends, the private coaching, the hope, the confusion. The coach may remember the player who looked capable in some situations and lost in others.

The discovery does not prove that colour blindness caused everything. It does not rewrite the whole career. But it can reveal that a hidden factor was never properly considered. That is enough to hurt. It is also enough to teach.

The lesson is not to panic and see colour blindness everywhere. The lesson is to add it to the list of possibilities when the pattern fits. If a child repeatedly struggles with colour-coded information, look closer. If a player’s performance changes dramatically with kit, bib, cone, light or surface conditions, look closer. If a child can name colours but still hesitates when colour must be used quickly, look closer. If a routine eye check has happened but colour vision was not specifically tested, ask about it.

The earlier this happens, the better. Early awareness does not guarantee a football dream. Nothing can. But it can protect confidence. It can help a child understand themselves. It can give parents better questions. It can give coaches better tools. It can reduce avoidable shame. It can allow the player to build reliable cues before fear becomes habit.

That is what nobody saw sooner: not a lack of effort, not a lack of care, not a lack of football intelligence, but a hidden picture problem sitting underneath visible mistakes. Once adults understand that possibility, they can stop turning every unexplained moment into a character judgement.

A child cannot explain a different picture if they have never seen through anyone else’s eyes. The adults have to learn to ask.

Chapter 5 — When Football Becomes a Colour Test

Nobody tells a child they are about to take a colour test when they arrive at football.

They are told to get their boots on. They are told to warm up, listen in, join their group, pass quicker, press earlier, scan before receiving and move into space. The cones are already out. The bibs are already being handed around. The coach has a session planned, the parents are on the side, the players are half-listening and the ball is already rolling before anyone has stopped to ask whether the important information is clear enough for every child to use.

That is how football becomes a colour test without meaning to.

It does not happen because coaches are cruel or careless. Most of the time, it happens because colour is convenient. Colour organises football quickly. It separates teams. It divides groups. It marks zones. It tells players where to run. It helps coaches explain drills. It makes tactical boards easier to follow. It makes apps and diagrams look clean. Colour is fast, and football loves anything that feels fast.

The problem is that colour is not equally fast for everyone.

For most players, “red team press” is a simple instruction. They hear it, see the red shirts or bibs, and react. For a colour-blind player, the instruction may not be so immediate. They may need to check which team is red, confirm the shirt against another cue, look for socks, numbers, direction or teammates’ voices, and only then act. That extra process may be tiny, but football is often decided by tiny delays. By the time the player is certain, the moment may already have moved.

The same thing can happen with cones. A coach says, “Dribble to the orange cone and turn back.” To the coach, the instruction is clear. The orange cone is sitting there on the grass. Most players can see it. The drill begins. One child slows down, runs to the wrong marker, cuts late or hesitates. It looks like they were not listening. It looks like they did not understand. It looks like they lack focus. But the child may have been trying to find orange against green grass, mud, low light or shadow while also controlling a ball and watching other players.

That is not a listening problem. It is a design problem.

This is the hidden trap inside colour-coded football. A drill can look simple while quietly asking one player to solve a visual puzzle before they can solve the football problem. The coach thinks they are training passing, scanning, pressing or dribbling. For the colour-blind player, the first task may be something else: find the colour, separate the bibs, trust the shirt, locate the cone, identify the team, work out whether the instruction is safe. Only after that can they play the football.

When that happens, the drill is no longer testing only football ability. It is testing colour access.

Kits are one of the biggest examples. Many people think a kit clash only happens when two teams wear obviously similar colours. But kit confusion can be more complicated than that. Shirts may look different in the changing room and still become difficult at speed. Two colours may seem separate in bright light but closer together under floodlights. Rain can darken fabric. Mud can cover socks. Shadows can flatten contrast. A goalkeeper shirt can sit too close to one team’s colours. A referee shirt can add another moving body into the visual picture. A player who relies heavily on socks or shorts may lose those cues when the pitch gets muddy.

Football is not seen from one perfect angle. A player rarely gets the clean front view that a coach, parent or referee might imagine. In a match, players see bodies from the side, from behind, through peripheral vision, while turning, while under pressure, while half-screened by other players. A shirt may be folded, stretched, shadowed or partly hidden. The player may only catch a glimpse of a sleeve, a sock, a back, a movement pattern or a colour patch before deciding.

That is why shirts alone are not enough. Shorts and socks matter. Numbers matter. Body shape matters. Voice matters. Direction matters. A kit that seems acceptable because the shirts are different may still be difficult if the overall moving picture is too close. A player does not pass to a shirt on a hanger. They pass to a moving human being inside a crowded game.

Bibs create another common problem. Grassroots teams often use whatever bibs are available. One group wears red, the other wears green. One wears orange, the other yellow. One wears blue, the other purple. One wears dark bibs over dark training tops. The coach may see different colours and assume the problem is solved. But different colours are not always different enough. What matters is not only the colour name. What matters is whether the teams separate quickly in real playing conditions.

A bib that works for one player may not work for another. A bib that works in the coach’s hand may not work when players are running. A bib that works in sunshine may not work in shade. A bib that works at walking pace may not work during a press. A bib that works for central vision may be harder in peripheral vision. The colour-blind player may not fail because they cannot see the bib at all. They may fail because the bib does not give them certainty quickly enough.

Cones and markers carry the same risk. Red, orange, green or pink cones may be easy for some players and unreliable for others, especially on grass or worn surfaces. Indoors, different floor colours can create new problems. On 3G or artificial surfaces, some markers may stand out while others become weak. In poor light, a cone can disappear enough to slow the player down. The coach may think the player missed an instruction. The player may have been searching for a marker the setup made harder to see.

Lines matter too. Pitch lines, training grids and coloured markings can help organise a session, but they can also create confusion if they rely on weak contrast. A coloured line on a surface may be obvious to one player and barely useful to another. When a drill asks players to stay inside a zone, defend a coloured area or run through a coloured gate, the player has to see that boundary quickly enough to use it.

Tactical boards can become colour tests as well. Many coaches use coloured magnets or dots to represent teams, positions and movements. That can be helpful, but only if colour is not the only cue. If one team is red magnets and the other is green, a colour-blind player may struggle to follow the explanation, especially if the coach speaks quickly or points while talking. Add numbers, initials, player names, shapes, arrows and positions, and the board becomes clearer for everyone.

Digital football is not immune either. Apps, analysis tools, training graphics, social media diagrams, substitution boards, score graphics and video clips often use colour as if everyone receives it the same way. Red arrows, green zones, blue dots, purple lines, heat maps and coloured overlays may look professional while still excluding some players from fast understanding. If the colour carries the meaning alone, the design is fragile.

The hidden problem is not that colour exists in football. Colour is useful. The problem is when football behaves as if colour is enough. For some players, colour needs support. It needs a second cue. It needs contrast, shape, number, name, position, direction or voice. When colour is supported, the information becomes more reliable. When colour stands alone, the player may be forced to guess.

Guessing is not a small thing in football. Guessing has consequences. Guess wrong in training and the coach may correct you. Guess wrong in a match and the other team may score. Guess wrong in a trial and someone may decide you are not ready. Guess wrong often enough and you may stop wanting the ball. That is how a colour test becomes a confidence test.

Number 12 knew that feeling, even before he knew why. He did not think, “This match has become an accidental colour test.” He thought he was failing. When the teams were clear, he could play closer to instinct. When the colours were harder, he checked more. When he checked more, he slowed down. When he slowed down, the coach saw hesitation. The football problem was visible. The colour problem was not.

That invisibility is what makes this issue dangerous. If a player trips over a broken cone, everyone sees the cone. If a player struggles because the cone blends into the surface, people see only the player. If a player cannot hear an instruction because the coach is too far away, adults may repeat it louder. If the player cannot use the colour instruction quickly enough, adults may repeat the same colour word again, as if volume can fix contrast.

It cannot.

The answer is not complicated, but it requires a change in habit. Coaches, clubs and schools need to stop asking only, “Are these colours different?” They need to ask, “Is the important information clear without relying on colour alone?” That question changes the design of a session.

Instead of “red team press,” say “red team, attacking the clubhouse, press.” Instead of “go to the orange cone,” say “go to the cone nearest the goal,” or “go to cone number two,” or “go to the tall cone.” Instead of “blue group rotate,” say “blue group, group one, rotate.” Instead of “defend the green gate,” say “defend the left-hand gate.” Instead of using only coloured magnets on a board, add player initials, numbers or positional labels. Instead of trusting that two kits are different because the colour names are different, check whether they separate in greyscale, in movement and in the real conditions of the match.

These adjustments do not make football less demanding. They remove unnecessary confusion so the real football demand can be judged. A player should still have to scan, pass, press, defend, communicate and compete. The opponent should still make the game hard. The drill should still challenge them. But the player should not be slowed down by an avoidable colour trap before the football problem even begins.

Clearer design also helps players who are not colour blind. Young players benefit from clearer instructions. Tired players benefit from stronger cues. Players under pressure benefit from names and numbers. Players who missed the first instruction benefit from zones described by position. Teams communicate better when they do not rely on vague colour shouts. A session that is clearer is usually faster, cleaner and easier to coach.

That is why colour-safe football should not be framed as special treatment. It is better design. It is the same logic as using a properly inflated ball, marking the pitch clearly, explaining the rules, checking the goals are safe or making sure players know which direction they are attacking. Football already accepts that environment affects performance. Colour clarity belongs in that same category.

The emotional difference for a player can be huge. When the setup is clear, the player spends less energy verifying the picture and more energy playing the game. They can scan for space instead of searching for the safe shirt. They can listen for pressure instead of worrying about the cone. They can focus on the pass instead of fearing another wrong-team mistake. They can begin to trust again.

This is especially important for players who have already been embarrassed. A child who has passed to the wrong team before may carry that memory into the next match. If the kits are unclear again, they may start worrying before the ball arrives. Their mind becomes crowded with checks. Shirt. Socks. Number. Direction. Voice. Is it safe? What if I get it wrong again? That worry wastes energy. It is not weakness. It is a learned response to uncertainty.

When football becomes a colour test, the player may be judged on the wrong exam. The report may say they lack awareness, but the hidden test was visual separation. The coach may say they need to be braver, but the hidden test was whether the pass was safe to trust. The parent may think the child is losing confidence, but the hidden test has been draining confidence for months.

The solution is not to remove colour from football. That would be unrealistic and unnecessary. The solution is to stop making colour carry the whole message. Use colour, but do not make colour the only door into the instruction. Use shirts, but check the full kit. Use cones, but choose ones that stand out and describe them another way. Use tactical boards, but add numbers and names. Use apps, but avoid colour-only meaning. Use bibs, but choose contrast that works in motion, not just in theory.

Football becomes fairer when the information is clear enough to use. Then the player’s ability can be judged more honestly. The pass can be corrected as a pass. The decision can be coached as a decision. The scan can be improved as a scan. The player can be challenged by football rather than trapped by colour.

Nobody tells a child they are taking a colour test at football. But too often, they are. Once adults understand that, the next step is simple.

Stop testing colour by accident.

Start coaching football on purpose.

Chapter 6 — Worry Steals Football Ability

The player was not looking at the ball when the ball came.

That was the part nobody saw.

From the sideline, it looked like he had switched off. The pass came into him, and for the smallest moment, his body did not move with the confidence people expected. The ball was there. The space was there. The pass was on. A teammate had pulled wide, an opponent was closing, and the coach could already see the next action in his head. Receive, turn, play. Simple.

But inside the player’s mind, the moment was not simple. Before he could think about the best football decision, he was trying to answer a different question.

Which shirt is safe?

That question arrived first. It arrived before the turn, before the forward pass, before the scan for pressure, before the coach’s instruction could settle into action. It arrived because the player had learned, through experience, that colours could not always be trusted quickly. He had learned that a wrong guess could become a shout, a substitution, a laugh, a label or a quiet car journey home. So when the ball came, his mind did what it had learned to do.

It checked.

The check may have lasted less than a second. To everyone watching, it looked like hesitation. To the player, it was protection. He checked the shirt. Then the socks. Then the direction of the run. Then the voice. Then the number. By the time he trusted the picture, the first pass had gone. The opponent had stepped in. The coach saw delay. The player felt the old shame rising again.

This is one of the least understood parts of colour blindness in football. The problem is not only what the player sees. It is what the player has to worry about while playing.

A colour-blind player may spend the whole game carrying questions other players answer automatically. Are the kits clear? Are the bibs safe? Is that my teammate or the opponent? Are the socks different enough? Is the goalkeeper shirt too close to our team? Is the referee adding confusion? Did the coach say orange or red? Is the cone on the far side the right one? Are the colours changing in the shade? Will I pass to the wrong player again? Will everyone think I am stupid if I get this wrong?

That is not normal football focus. It is colour doubt sitting inside the game.

Football already demands enough from a player. They have to scan, move, communicate, receive, pass, defend, press, track runners, read body shape, judge space, protect the ball, manage pressure, listen to the coach, stay aware of the score and make decisions in moments that vanish almost as soon as they appear. A young player has to do all of that while also managing emotion: fear of mistakes, desire to impress, pressure from parents, pressure from teammates, pressure from selection and pressure from their own dream.

Now add a hidden visual question underneath it all: can I trust the picture?

That question steals energy.

It does not always steal energy dramatically. The player does not collapse. They do not suddenly stop running. They may still look fit. They may still try hard. But mental energy is being spent in a place others cannot see. Every extra check costs something. A little focus. A little speed. A little confidence. A little freedom. Over one pass, it may not seem like much. Over a full match, it can change how a player looks.

The player may appear nervous because they are never fully relaxed in the picture. They may appear slow because certainty is arriving late. They may appear negative because safer passes feel less risky than forward passes through colour confusion. They may appear inconsistent because some kit combinations allow them to play freely and others force them into checking. They may appear tired because decision-making has been carrying an extra weight from the first whistle.

This is not weakness. It is cognitive load.

Cognitive load is the work the brain has to do to process information. In football, every player carries some cognitive load. The game is complex. But when a colour-blind player has to keep verifying basic visual cues that others receive quickly, their load increases before the football decision even begins. They are not only deciding what to do with the ball. They are deciding which information is safe enough to trust.

That is where paradox of choice enters the game. For most players, colour simplifies the picture. It reduces options. It says: this shirt is safe, that shirt is danger; this cone is the target, that cone is not; this team presses, that team keeps possession. Colour narrows the decision quickly. For a colour-blind player, colour may do the opposite. It may create more questions.

Instead of one clear cue, the player has to weigh many possible cues. Shirt colour. Sock colour. Shorts. Number. Voice. Position. Movement. Direction of attack. Body shape. Space. The coach’s instruction. The memory of what the teams looked like before kick-off. The fear of the previous mistake. The nearest safe pass. The risk of being shouted at. Too many cues can crowd the moment. The player is not simply choosing the best football action. They are first choosing which clue to believe.

That crowding can interrupt flow. Flow in football is not magic. It is the state where the player is absorbed in the game, reading and acting without feeling trapped in self-conscious thought. A player in flow does not ask permission from every cue. They see, trust and play. But worry breaks that chain. Worry adds commentary. Worry says, Are you sure? Check again. Remember last time. Do not get it wrong. Do not embarrass yourself. Do not give them another reason to doubt you.

When that voice is active, the player is no longer fully inside the football. Part of them is watching themselves play.

That can be devastating for confidence. A player who does not trust the picture may stop trusting their instincts. A player who stops trusting instinct may look less natural. A player who looks less natural may receive more criticism. More criticism creates more worry. More worry creates more checking. More checking creates more delay. The cycle feeds itself.

Number 12 knew that cycle. Before he understood colour blindness, he thought the problem was inside his character. He thought he needed to be braver, sharper, calmer, quicker. Sometimes those things were true in the ordinary football sense. Every player needs improvement. But there was something else underneath. He was not only trying to improve. He was trying to stop being exposed by a picture that changed without warning.

Some matches felt safe. In those games, he could play closer to instinct. The teams separated, the cues were clear, the ball moved, and for a while he felt like the player he wanted to be. Other matches felt unsafe from the start. The kits were closer. The light was strange. Socks became muddy. Opponents moved through shadows. The ball arrived and his mind started checking before his body could trust.

That inconsistency became its own punishment. It is hard enough for a child to struggle. It is harder when the struggle comes and goes. If a player is always poor at something, adults know what to train. But when a player looks good one week and lost the next, people often reach for personality explanations. He is inconsistent. She lacks focus. He cannot handle pressure. She does not want it enough. The player may begin to believe the same thing, because they do not have a better explanation.

Worry also changes risk. Football development requires risk. Players have to try passes they might miss, receive under pressure, turn into space, switch play, take responsibility, ask for the ball and make decisions before they are guaranteed to work. A child who fears visual mistakes may start avoiding those moments. Not because they do not care. Because they care too much. The risk is not only losing the ball. The risk is being misunderstood again.

A wrong pass caused by poor judgement is painful, but it belongs to football. A wrong pass caused by a visual cue the player could not trust is different. It feels unfair in a way the child may not be able to explain. The player feels punished for a problem nobody else can see. After enough of those moments, safe football starts to feel sensible. Keep it simple. Do not switch it. Do not play through the crowd. Do not receive on the half-turn. Do not risk the pass if the shirt is not certain. Do not give them another reason.

From the outside, the player may look like they lack ambition. Inside, they may be trying to avoid shame.

This matters for parents because worry is often visible before the reason is. A parent may see the child become tense before certain games. They may notice the child asking about kits, bibs or who is on which team. They may see a child who once demanded the ball begin to hide. They may see frustration after matches where the child says little but looks heavy. They may hear, “I don’t know,” when they ask what happened. It is tempting to treat this as confidence alone. But confidence may be responding to a hidden visual pattern.

Parents should listen for the worry underneath the football. Does the child worry about passing to the wrong player? Do they talk about colours being confusing? Do they say certain bibs look the same? Do they dislike particular drills? Do they play better when teams are clearly separated? Do they seem mentally tired after games where kits or cones were difficult? These questions do not diagnose colour blindness, but they can reveal whether colour uncertainty is draining the child’s focus.

Coaches should look for the same thing in behaviour. A player carrying colour worry may check more than others. They may need extra confirmation before passing. They may look around at teammates before starting a drill. They may ask repeated questions about teams. They may become quiet after a wrong-shirt mistake. They may avoid receiving in crowded areas. They may play quickly in one setup and cautiously in another. They may seem to improve immediately when the bibs, cones or instructions become clearer.

That last point is important. If changing the environment changes the player, the environment was part of the performance. A coach who notices that can help without drama. They do not need to announce anything. They can simply make the session clearer. Better bibs. Clearer cones. Names before passes. Numbers on teams. Position-based instructions. Greyscale kit checks. Directional cues. A private question after training. Small changes can reduce the hidden workload.

The player also needs tools. Worry becomes less powerful when there is a plan. If the kits are confusing, the player can be taught to use numbers, socks, shorts and voices. If cones are unreliable, the coach can number them or describe them by position. If the player fears wrong-team passes, teammates can call names earlier. If the player feels uncertainty rising, they can use a reset action: bounce pass, communicate, scan again, find the next clear cue. A plan does not remove all pressure, but it turns worry into strategy.

That shift is emotionally important. A player who thinks, “I panic,” is trapped inside identity. A player who thinks, “I need a clearer cue,” has something to do. A player who thinks, “I am slow,” may shrink. A player who thinks, “I was checking the shirt because the colours were close,” can learn a better system. The goal is not to excuse the player from improvement. The goal is to give the player the right improvement.

This is where adults must be careful with language. Words like “panic,” “scared,” “lazy,” “slow” and “not brave” may feel like ordinary football criticism, but to a child carrying hidden visual worry, they can become identity. The child may not hear, “This action needs improving.” They may hear, “This is who I am.” Better language keeps the door open. What did you see? What cue were you using? Was the picture clear? Did you need to check? What can we make easier to identify next time? What is your reset if you are unsure?

Those questions give the player room to tell the truth.

Worry is also one of the reasons early discovery matters so much. If a child understands colour blindness earlier, they can learn trusted cues before worry becomes habit. They can build confidence around what works. They can explain themselves privately. They can stop turning every uncertain moment into self-blame. Parents can support the right issue. Coaches can design clearer sessions. The player still has to play football, but they are no longer fighting an unnamed opponent.

Chapter 7 — The Half-Second Problem

In football, doubt is expensive.

It does not need to last long to change the moment. A player may only hesitate for the smallest beat, the kind of delay that disappears on a clock but not on a pitch. A defender steps closer. A passing lane narrows. A teammate’s run goes from open to blocked. The ball that could have been played first time now needs an extra touch. The extra touch invites pressure. The pressure turns a simple action into a rushed one. The rushed action becomes a mistake. The mistake becomes the evidence people use to judge the player.

From the sideline, it looks like the player was late. Inside the moment, the player may have been trying to become certain.

That distinction matters. Football is a game of recognition before action. The best players do not only run faster or kick cleaner. They recognise earlier. They identify the teammate, the opponent, the space, the danger, the spare player, the pressing trigger and the next pass before the moment closes. Speed in football is not only physical. It is visual, mental and emotional. A player who recognises the picture early can play quickly. A player who has to verify the picture first may look slower even when their body is capable.

For a colour-blind player, the half-second problem can begin before the ball arrives. The player may already be scanning, but the information they scan may not separate cleanly. They may need to check whether the moving shirt belongs to their team. They may need to use socks because shirts are unreliable. They may need to wait for a voice. They may need the number to turn. They may need to confirm the direction of the run. These are intelligent strategies, but they take time.

Football often gives no time back.

This chapter is not claiming that every colour-blind player loses an exact measured half-second in every situation. That would be too precise and too broad. Different players, conditions and colour combinations will vary. The point is more practical and more familiar to anyone who has played the game: even a brief hesitation can matter. If the picture takes longer to trust, the football action may arrive late enough to be judged differently.

A late action changes the story. A first-time pass can look confident. The same pass after an extra touch can look nervous. A forward pass played early can break a line. The same pass played late can be intercepted. A player who receives and turns before pressure arrives looks aware. A player who checks, delays and gets tackled looks slow. The difference may be tiny, but football is full of tiny differences that become large consequences.

This is why colour blindness in football cannot be understood only by asking whether the player eventually identifies the correct colour. Eventually is not enough. Football asks for now. A player may identify the shirt after the pass has gone. They may find the cone after the turn should have happened. They may recognise the teammate after the lane has closed. They may understand the instruction after the drill has already moved past them.

Late recognition can look like poor ability when it is really delayed certainty.

Number 12 lived in that gap. He was not always confused for long. Often, he was almost there. Almost sure. Almost ready. Almost trusting the pass. That almost was painful because it made the mistake look worse. If he had been completely lost, perhaps adults would have asked different questions. But he was close enough that the mistake looked like a failure of sharpness. He saw something, but not quickly enough. He recognised something, but not soon enough. He acted, but the game had already moved.

That is the cruel part of the half-second problem. It does not always announce itself as confusion. It can look like a player who is nearly good enough but not quite reliable. Nearly quick enough. Nearly brave enough. Nearly aware enough. The player becomes trapped in the space between ability and trust.

Coaches know how much timing matters. They spend entire sessions trying to improve it. Play in two touches. Move the ball quicker. Scan before receiving. Arrive on time. Press as the ball travels. Release before pressure comes. Break lines early. Recognise the trigger. Football language is full of timing because the game punishes delay. Yet colour clarity is often left out of that timing conversation.

A coach may demand quicker play while unknowingly designing delay into the session. If bibs are difficult to separate, the player’s first task is not the pass. It is identification. If cones are unclear, the player’s first task is not the dribble. It is finding the target. If the tactical board relies only on colour, the player’s first task is not understanding movement. It is decoding the magnets. If the kits are close, the player’s first task is not breaking pressure. It is deciding who is safe.

This is not about making football easy. It is about making the right thing difficult. The opponent should make football hard. The speed of play should challenge the player. Tactical problems should stretch them. Pressure should test them. But avoidable visual confusion should not be mistaken for the challenge. A player should be late because they failed to scan, not because the information they scanned was unclear. A player should lose the ball because the defender pressed well, not because the teams were difficult to separate.

The half-second problem also affects confidence because it repeats in a way the player may not understand. A player who is late once may accept the correction. A player who keeps being late in certain conditions begins to feel there is something wrong with them. They may not notice that the delay appears more often with particular kits, surfaces, lights or bibs. They may simply feel unreliable.

Adults may not notice either. If the same player performs better when teams are visually clear, that improvement may be treated as a good day rather than a clue. If they struggle when colours are close, that struggle may be treated as a bad day rather than a pattern. Over time, the child’s performance record becomes mixed. Good moments keep the dream alive. Bad moments keep trust away.

In selection environments, this matters. Trials, academy games and tournaments often judge players in short windows. A coach may only see a child for one match, one session or one half. A brief hesitation at the wrong time can shape an impression. A player who needs extra checking because of unclear kit contrast may be judged as slow to process. A player who plays safe because they do not trust shirt cues may be judged as lacking bravery. A player who misidentifies a teammate may be judged as having poor awareness.

This does not mean colour blindness explains every missed selection. It does not. Selection is complex and often brutal. Many players are not selected for reasons that have nothing to do with colour vision. But when football environments rely heavily on colour, it is reasonable to ask whether some players are being judged in conditions that make their ability harder to see. That question is not an excuse. It is a fairness concern.

The half-second problem is not only about passing. It can appear in defending. A defender who hesitates because the runner is not instantly identified may lose a yard. A midfielder who checks a shirt before pressing may arrive late. A winger who cannot find the correct cone in a speed drill may look slower than they are. A goalkeeper who has to separate shirts quickly in a crowded box may face extra uncertainty. A substitute trying to understand a tactical board may miss detail if colours are the only cue.

It can also appear in communication. If a player does not trust the visual picture, they may rely more heavily on voices. That can help, but only if teammates communicate early and clearly. A late call may not be enough. “Man on,” “turn,” “set,” “time,” “switch,” and names can become essential anchors. A team that communicates well gives the colour-blind player more ways to trust the moment. A quiet team leaves the player alone with the picture.

Parents may see the effect as hesitation. They may watch their child receive the ball, pause, and then make a decision that seems late. They may hear the coach shout for quicker play. They may think the child lacks confidence. That may be part of it, but parents should also ask whether the hesitation changes with visual conditions. Was the child quicker when kits were clear? Did they struggle more when both teams had dark socks? Did floodlights make it worse? Did the child ask questions about teams before kick-off? Did they seem anxious about passing to the wrong player?

Those questions matter because the half-second problem is easier to reduce when adults understand it. If the player’s delay comes from uncertainty, give them certainty earlier. Identify the strongest cues before the match. Tell the player which socks, shorts, numbers or directional cues to trust. Ask teammates to call names sooner. Use clearer bibs in training. Avoid colour-only instructions. Check whether the goalkeeper or referee kit adds confusion. Use a greyscale photo to see whether two kits become too similar in tone.

For the player, the answer is not to panic about every colour. The answer is to build a hierarchy of cues. If shirts are clear, use shirts. If shirts are not clear, use socks and shorts. If the full kit is unclear, use numbers and voices. If the picture is crowded, use direction of attack, position and movement. If uncertain under pressure, use a safe reset action rather than freezing: bounce pass, call a name, play into space, or protect the ball and communicate.

The goal is to reduce the checking time before the moment arrives. The player should not be forced to invent the cue during the decision. The cue should be planned earlier. This is why pre-match and pre-session clarity matters. Waiting until the player has already made the mistake is too late. By then, the damage is public. The shout has come. The confidence has taken another hit.

A coach can help by turning clarity into habit. Before a session, check whether the teams and cones are clear. Before a match, look at the kits together, not separately. Before a tactical explanation, make sure the board uses more than colour. Before criticising a late pass, ask what information the player had. These habits do not take long. In many cases, they take less time than repairing the damage caused by misunderstanding.

The emotional repair is important. A player who has been judged for late decisions may need to relearn trust. They may need repeated experiences where the picture is clear, the cue works, and the decision succeeds. They may need a coach to say, “Good idea, wrong shirt. Let’s fix the cue,” instead of treating the whole action as poor football. That kind of language separates the player’s football intelligence from the visual confusion. It gives them something to improve without making them feel defective.

The half-second problem can also affect the player’s relationship with risk. The player who knows they are slightly delayed may try to avoid situations where delay will be exposed. They may stop receiving under pressure. They may avoid central areas. They may not make forward runs that require quick identification from teammates. They may become a player who stays safe because safe gives them more time. That can limit development. Football growth often requires being willing to act before certainty feels perfect. But if colour uncertainty has punished the player before, caution makes sense.

This is why clarity is not only about one pass. It is about the player’s whole development environment. If a child learns in a clearer setup, they can spend more time developing football habits and less time surviving visual doubt. They can learn to scan earlier because the scan produces useful information. They can learn to play quickly because the cue is trustworthy. They can learn bravery because risk is no longer mixed with avoidable confusion.

The half-second problem should not make adults fearful. It should make them practical. A brief hesitation matters, so reduce avoidable hesitation. Give players more than one cue. Improve contrast. Ask better questions. Prepare before the mistake. If colour is unreliable, do not leave the player to solve it alone in the fastest moments of the game.

Football will always punish delay. That is part of the sport. But it should punish football delay, not hidden visual delay that could have been reduced with clearer design.

In football, doubt is expensive. Clarity gives the player a better chance to pay attention to the game instead of the uncertainty in the picture.

A child may seem slow on a task that depends on colour, but fine on the same topic when labels or shapes are added. They may dislike subjects or activities where colour mistakes are visible. They may be embarrassed, especially if other children notice. A parent may hear small comments from teachers without realising they matter.

In football, the signs can be sharper because the game is faster and more public. A child may pass to the wrong team when kits or bibs are similar. They may hesitate before passing. They may hold the ball too long. They may play backwards when a forward pass is available. They may miss cones in drills, especially red or orange cones on grass. They may ask who is on which team more often than others. They may track numbers, socks or shorts instead of shirts. They may look confident in clear training sessions but unsure in crowded matches. They may worry before games where the kits are similar. They may become quiet after being shouted at for a mistake that looked obvious to everyone else.

The parent may feel confused because the child is not always struggling. That inconsistency can delay understanding. If the child struggled every time, the problem might be easier to investigate. But colour-related difficulty often depends on the environment. One match has clear kits and good light; another has muddy socks, shadows and similar shirts. One coach uses names and numbers; another uses colour-only instructions. One surface makes cones stand out; another swallows them. The player changes with the picture, and adults call the player inconsistent.

A parent can begin by asking after the game, not accusing during it. The most useful question is not, “Why did you do that?” It is, “What did you see?” That question gives the child room to describe the moment without immediately defending themselves. It may take time for the answer to come. A child who has been criticised often may not trust the question at first. They may expect a lecture. They may shrug. They may say, “I don’t know.” The parent’s job is to keep the door open.

Useful questions sound calm and specific. Were the kits easy to tell apart? Were the bibs clear? Could you see the cone quickly? Did the socks help? Did the shadows make it harder? Did you know who was on your team when you passed? Were you worried about passing to the wrong player? Did this happen in training too? Which colours are hardest? Do numbers help? Do voices help? Did you feel unsure before the mistake?

These questions are not about giving the child an excuse. They are about finding the information that came before the action. Sometimes the child will say, “No, I just made a bad pass.” That is useful too. Then the football can be coached as football. But sometimes the child will say something that changes the picture. “I thought he was on my team.” “The bibs looked close.” “I couldn’t see the cone.” “I wasn’t sure.” “I hate when we wear those colours.” “I was scared I’d do it again.”

Those answers deserve attention.

Parents must also be careful with emotion. Discovering a possible explanation can bring relief, but it can also bring fear. A parent may suddenly remember years of signs and feel guilty for missing them. They may feel angry at coaches, schools or themselves. They may want to announce the issue loudly so nobody misunderstands the child again. Those feelings are human, but the child’s dignity must come first.

A child should not become a public project. They should not be announced to the team without consent. They should not be made to feel broken. They should not hear adults speak about them as if their football dream is over. The safest message to the child is calm: “This might explain some things. It does not mean you cannot play. It means we can understand the picture better and help you find cues that work.”

That message matters because children often hear more than adults intend. A parent who panics may make the child panic. A parent who treats colour blindness as a disaster may teach the child to feel ashamed. A parent who treats it as useful information can help the child feel less alone. The tone should be serious but not frightened. Practical, not pitying. Curious, not accusing.

The next step is proper testing. If colour vision is a concern, parents should speak to a qualified eye-care professional and ask specifically about colour vision testing. A standard eyesight check and a colour vision test are not always the same thing. A child may also have poor eyesight, blurred vision, headaches, light sensitivity or other visual issues that affect sport. Those should be checked properly too, not guessed at from the touchline.

This distinction is important because eyesight and colour vision are different questions. A child may need glasses or contact lenses for sharpness, and may also have colour vision deficiency. A child may pass an eyesight chart and still have colour-related difficulty. A child may complain of headaches or blurred vision and need professional assessment beyond anything a parent or coach can provide. The book can guide questions, but it cannot replace an examination.

When parents speak to coaches, the conversation should be private and practical. Many parents worry they will sound like they are making excuses. That fear is understandable, especially in competitive football where parents are often judged too. But a well-framed conversation is not an excuse. It is performance information.

A parent might say, “We are looking into possible colour vision issues. We are not asking you to lower standards, but we have noticed he sometimes struggles when bibs or kits are close. It helps when instructions include names, numbers or positions as well as colour. Could we keep an eye on whether certain colours or setups make things harder?”

That kind of conversation gives the coach something useful. It does not demand special treatment. It does not blame the coach. It does not diagnose the child publicly. It simply opens the door to better observation and clearer cues.

Parents should also ask the child what they are comfortable sharing. Some children may be happy for the coach to know. Some may not want teammates told. Some may want only small changes without attention. Teenagers especially may fear being seen as different or weak. Respecting that matters. The child should have some control over who knows and how it is handled, unless there is a safety or safeguarding reason that requires adult action.

The best support often looks invisible. The coach uses better bibs. The team calls names earlier. Cones are described by position. Tactical boards use numbers. The child gets tested properly. The parent watches patterns. Nobody makes a dramatic announcement. The environment improves, and the child is allowed to keep their dignity.

Parents can also help their child build practical cues. Before a match, look at the kits together if appropriate. Ask which parts are easiest to separate: shirts, socks, shorts, numbers, direction or goalkeeper colours. Encourage the child to use names and voices. Help them create a reset line after mistakes: “Find the next clear cue.” Remind them that one wrong pass does not define them. If they are unsure, they can use a safer action while communicating: bounce pass, protect the ball, call a name, play to space, or reset.

The point is not to make the child obsessed with colour. The point is to reduce fear by giving them a plan. Worry grows when the child feels helpless. Confidence grows when they know what to do.

Parents should also protect the child from regret becoming identity. If colour blindness is discovered late, there may be grief. The child may wonder what would have happened if they had known earlier. The parent may wonder the same. Those feelings are real, especially if years of football investment, money, travel, missed work and emotional sacrifice are involved. But the message must remain balanced. Colour blindness may explain some things, but it does not explain everything. It may have made parts of football harder, but it does not make the child a failure. The purpose of knowing now is to help from this point forward.

For younger children, that may mean adapting while the dream is still alive. For older players, it may mean understanding the past, rebuilding confidence and playing with more freedom. For parents, it may mean forgiving themselves for not knowing what nobody had taught them to look for. Awareness should not become a weapon turned backwards. It should become a tool used forwards.

The parent who notices first can change the story. Not by diagnosing, not by blaming, and not by shouting louder than the coach, but by asking the question nobody else has asked. What did you see? When does it happen? Which colours are hardest? What helps? Has colour vision been tested? How can we make the game clearer without embarrassing the child?

Those questions can protect confidence before it breaks. They can help a coach see the pattern. They can help a child stop blaming themselves for every unclear moment. They can turn years of confusion into a practical plan.

A parent does not need perfect knowledge to begin. They need curiosity, calmness and the courage to look beyond the obvious explanation. Sometimes that is enough to help the next child earlier than Number 12 was helped.

Chapter 10 — Coach the Game, Not the Confusion

The pass went to the wrong shirt, and the coach saw it.

Everyone saw it.

The player saw it too. He knew before the shout came. His shoulders dropped before the coach opened his mouth, because his body already knew the sound that usually followed a mistake like that.

“What are you doing?”

“Wake up.”

“You have to see that.”

But this time, imagine the coach stopping for one second. Not long enough for the whole session to pause. Not long enough for the parents to notice. Just long enough to look again. The shirts were muddier now than they had been at kick-off. The low sun had flattened the colours. The two teams were moving in and out of shadow. The socks were not as different as they had looked when the players were standing still. The mistake was still a mistake, but the picture around the mistake had changed.

The coach felt the old words rise, then swallowed them. He called the player over and asked quietly, “What did you see?”

That is where better coaching begins.

Not with excuses. Not with softer standards. Not with letting mistakes go. Better coaching begins with better diagnosis, because a coach cannot fix the right problem if they name the wrong one.

A coach does not need to be an eye specialist to change a child’s football life. Sometimes the difference is much smaller than that. A different bib. A clearer cone. A name shouted earlier. A number used instead of a colour. A private question instead of a public accusation. A five-second pause before training starts, where the coach looks at the setup and asks whether every player can actually see what they are being asked to do.

That question can protect a player. It can protect confidence, talent and trust. It can stop a football problem from becoming a personal wound. The coach often has more power than they realise because the coach controls so much of the picture. The coach chooses the bibs. The coach places the cones. The coach explains the drill. The coach sets the standard. The coach decides whether colour is the only instruction or one cue among many. The coach decides whether a mistake becomes a teaching moment or a label.

For a colour-blind player, two coaching responses can lead to very different futures. “Wake up” closes the child down. “What did you see?” opens the truth.

This does not mean coaches are responsible for diagnosing colour blindness. They are not. Diagnosis belongs with qualified professionals. It also does not mean coaches should treat colour-blind players as fragile or excuse poor play. Football still needs standards. A player still needs to scan, communicate, pass properly, make brave decisions, compete and learn. But if the visual setup is unclear, the coach may be correcting the wrong problem.

A player who passes late may need technical work. They may need to scan earlier. They may need to open their body. They may need to make better decisions. But they may also have needed an extra moment to separate teammate from opponent. A player who misses a cone may not be ignoring instructions; the cone may not stand out from the surface. A player who plays safe may not lack bravery; they may have learned that guessing wrong brings shame. A player who looks inconsistent may be reacting to an environment that is inconsistent.

This is the coaching challenge: not to excuse every mistake, but to diagnose more accurately.

Good coaches already understand context. They know a player’s touch can change under pressure. They know fatigue affects decisions. They know confidence affects movement. They know the pitch, weather, opposition and match state can alter performance. Colour clarity belongs in that same category. It is part of the playing environment. A coach would not run a passing drill in darkness and then blame the players for poor awareness. A coach would not use a flat ball and then judge first touch fairly. A coach would not place broken cones in a confusing pattern and call the children stupid for misunderstanding the drill. So why judge a player inside a visually unclear colour setup as if the setup has no effect?

The wrong setup can create the wrong player.

Put a child in a clear environment and they may play quickly. They scan, turn, pass, call for the ball and look like themselves. Change the bibs, the cones, the light or the kit contrast, and the same child may hesitate. They take an extra touch. They play backwards. They look unsure. They stop trusting what they see. To the coach, it may look like inconsistency. To the player, it feels like betrayal. The game that made sense ten minutes ago no longer feels reliable.

If the coach judges only the second version, the child gets labelled by the environment. Slow. Careless. Nervous. Weak. Inconsistent. But what if the player is not inconsistent? What if the setup is?

That question matters because children become what adults repeatedly call them. A player who is told they are slow may stop trying to play quickly. A player who is told they are careless may stop trusting their decisions. A player who is told they lack bravery may stop asking for the ball. Repeated wrong explanations become identity. Coaching language matters not because children cannot handle correction, but because correction should aim at the real problem.

The coach’s new first question should be simple: what information did the player have when they made the decision? That is a better question than “Why did he do that?” because football decisions are not made in empty space. A player receives a picture, sorts it, trusts or doubts it, then acts. If the picture was clear, the coach can judge the action more fairly. If the picture was unclear, the action may have been solving the wrong problem.

The player may not have been thinking, “How do I break the press?” They may have been thinking, “Which shirt is safe?” They may not have been thinking, “Can I play forward?” They may have been thinking, “Is that my teammate?” That hidden question slows the real football question.

This is not about removing pressure from football. Pressure belongs in the game. The coach’s job is not to remove the opponent, the challenge or the demand for speed. The coach’s job is to remove the pressure that should never have been there: avoidable confusion caused by unclear information.

Some coaches may worry that this makes football too soft. It does not. Clarity is not a lowering of standards. It is what allows standards to mean something. A player should still be challenged to scan, pass quickly, communicate, compete, receive under pressure and make brave decisions. But the correction should come after the visual setup has been checked. If a coach keeps correcting a football problem that is actually a visibility problem, everyone loses. The player loses confidence, the coach loses trust, the team loses clarity, and the real football lesson gets buried.

A coach cannot demand speed while designing delay into the session.

Before any drill, the coach can use a simple clarity habit. Look at the setup and ask: can every player understand the task without relying on colour alone? Can the teams separate clearly? Can the cones be seen from distance and at speed? Can zones be recognised without colour-only instructions? Can the goalkeeper be separated from both teams? Can the players understand the task if they miss the colour word? Can the same instruction work through numbers, names, shapes, directions or positions?

If the answer is no, the session is not ready. Not because it is impossible, but because it is unnecessarily unclear. The coach does not need perfect equipment. They can still improve the picture. Move the cone. Change the bib. Add a number. Use a shape. Give the zone a name. Use left and right. Use near and far. Use player names. Use positions. Use voice. Make the important information available in more than one way.

Bibs are one of the easiest problems to fix and one of the easiest problems to ignore. A coach may think different colours are enough, but different colours are not always different enough. Red against green, red against orange, green against yellow, bright green against orange, blue against purple, dark against dark, and any combination that becomes similar in greyscale can create problems for some players. Safer thinking is simple: use strong light-dark contrast, use clearer combinations where possible, use numbers if available, and if equipment is limited, add non-colour cues.

A team with bibs can attack one direction. A team without bibs can attack the other. One team can call names before receiving. Bibs can be numbered. Groups can have role names. The answer does not always need to be expensive. It needs to be visible.

Cones can turn football into a colour test very quickly. A coach says, “Dribble to the orange cone.” Most players go. One player slows down. The coach thinks the child has not listened. But maybe the child is trying to find orange on grass, in mud, under shadow, while controlling the ball. That is not a listening problem. That is a design problem. Instead of “go to the orange cone,” say “go to the cone nearest the goal,” “go to cone number two,” “go to the tall cone,” “go to the left gate,” or “go to the cone I am standing beside.” The moment the coach gives a second cue, the player no longer has to solve colour before solving football.

Coaching language is another hidden trap. Coaches use colour because it is quick. “Red team press.” “Green cone turn.” “Blue group rotate.” “Orange zone is out.” “Pass to yellow.” For many players, that is fine. For some, it adds friction. The solution is not to ban colour words completely. The solution is to stop making colour the only route to understanding. Say colour plus role, colour plus direction, colour plus number, colour plus name, colour plus shape. “Red team, the team attacking the clubhouse, press.” “Green cone, the far-left cone, is the turn.” “Blue group, group one, rotate.” “Orange zone, the square nearest me, is out.”

This helps more than colour-blind players. It helps younger players, tired players, distracted players, players under pressure, players with English as a second language and players who missed the first instruction. Clarity helps the whole team.

Privacy matters just as much as practicality. One of the biggest mistakes a coach can make is turning a hidden difficulty into public attention. A child may already feel embarrassed. They may not want the team to know. They may not want changes made “because of them.” They may not even have the language to explain it properly. Do not announce, “We have to change the bibs because he can’t see colours.” That may humiliate a child and make them hide more.

A better sentence is, “We are going to make the setup clearer for everyone.” That is enough. The coach can change bibs without explaining publicly. The coach can add numbers without naming the player. The coach can use clearer instructions without turning one child into the reason. If a private conversation is needed, keep it calm: “I noticed some colours seemed harder to separate today. No problem. We can make it clearer. Tell me privately if certain bibs or cones are difficult.”

A child who feels safe is more likely to tell the truth. A child who feels exposed is more likely to hide.

Coaches can use a simple observation loop. First, observe when the struggle appears. Does the player struggle only in certain bibs, low light, crowded games, or colour-coded drills? Second, ask what changed. Did the equipment, light, kit, surface or instruction change? Third, adjust the visual design. Change bibs, move cones, add numbers, use names, increase contrast or reduce colour-only cues. Fourth, retest the football. Does the player play quicker, ask for the ball more, pass earlier or look more confident? Fifth, coach the football properly. Once the picture is clearer, demand the standard.

The first words after a mistake matter. Instead of “What are you doing?” try “What did you see?” Instead of “You have to see that pass,” try “What cue were you using?” Instead of “You’re too slow,” try “Did the picture feel clear or did you need to check?” Instead of “You’re not listening,” try “Was the cone easy to find?” These questions do not excuse poor play. They reveal information. Sometimes the answer will be, “I just made a bad pass.” Fine. Coach the pass. Sometimes the answer will be, “I thought he was on my team.” Now the coach has learned something important.

The coach should also watch for the player who plays smaller than their ability. Not always, and not everywhere, but in certain visual conditions. The player stops taking risks, stops demanding the ball, passes backwards too quickly or holds it too long. The coach may think the player needs more confidence. That may be true, but confidence is not created by shouting “be confident.” Confidence grows when the player trusts the picture enough to act. If the player has repeatedly been embarrassed by wrong-team passes, safe football may be self-protection. The child is not only avoiding risk. They are avoiding shame.

A good coach helps rebuild trust by creating clearer information and then giving controlled moments to act. “Your cue is the number on the bib.” “Your first look is wide right.” “If you are unsure, bounce pass and move.” “Listen for the early call.” “Use socks and shorts if shirts clash.” “After one mistake, reset straight away.” Bravery becomes easier when the player has a plan.

Coaches should not wait for diagnosis before improving clarity. Most grassroots teams will not know who is colour blind. Many children will not know. Some players will hide it. Some parents will be unsure. The best approach is universal clarity. Design sessions as if someone may struggle with colour, because someone probably will, now or in the future. This is not special treatment. It is better design.

On match day, the coach may not control the opposition kit, the weather, the referee’s shirt or the pitch. But they can still prepare. Before kick-off, look at both teams together. Do the shirts separate? Do the shorts and socks separate? Does the goalkeeper clash? Does the referee blend with either team? Will mud make this worse? Will floodlights or low sun make this worse? Would the kits still be clear in greyscale? If there is a risk, act early where possible. Use alternate bibs if allowed. Ask about changing tops. Make players call names loudly. Tell the colour-blind player privately which cues are strongest.

When a parent tells a coach their child may be colour blind, the response matters. The parent may worry the coach will think they are making excuses. They may worry the child will be treated differently. The best response is calm and practical: “Thank you for telling me. We will not make a big deal of it publicly. I can make the session clearer for everyone by using stronger contrast, names, numbers and non-colour cues. Let me know which colours or situations seem hardest.”

Do not say, “He seems fine to me,” “He knows his colours,” “We cannot change everything for one player,” or “He just needs to concentrate.” Those responses close the door. The coach does not need to promise perfection. Just curiosity and practical action.

If a player tells a coach directly, protect that moment. They may have needed courage to say it, especially if they are older, competitive, or afraid of being judged. A good response is, “Thanks for telling me. That helps me coach you better. You are not asking for easier football. We will make the cues clearer so you can play quicker.” Then ask privately which colours are hardest, whether cones, bibs or kits are the main problem, whether numbers help, and who the player is comfortable telling.

That last point matters. The player should have some control over who knows. Dignity builds trust. Trust builds honesty. Honesty builds better coaching.

The best coaches do not only improve players. They improve the conditions in which players are judged. They know that standards matter, but clarity comes first. They understand that the environment can lie about ability. They refuse to let poor visual design pretend to be poor football.

Somewhere, there is a coach who will read this and remember a player. The quiet one. The inconsistent one. The one who looked brilliant sometimes and lost at others. The one who played well until bibs came out. The one who held the ball too long. The one who passed to the wrong team and stared at the ground. The one who stopped asking for it.

Maybe that player was lazy. Maybe they lacked confidence. Maybe they needed technical work. But maybe there was another question nobody asked.

What did they see?

That question may come late for some players. But it can arrive on time for the next one.

  • Clearer cues do not lower standards; they make standards fairer and more accurate.

Chapter 11 — How Players Find Trust Again

Seeing clearly is the beginning. Playing freely is the goal.

For a player who has spent months or years doubting the picture, discovery does not fix everything at once. Finding out about colour blindness can bring relief, but it can also bring anger, embarrassment, sadness and regret. The player may finally understand why certain things felt harder, but understanding the reason does not instantly remove the habits that grew around it. A player who has learned to check twice does not automatically trust the first look. A player who has learned to play safe does not suddenly become brave because the problem has a name. A player who has carried shame does not drop it in a single conversation.

Trust has to be rebuilt.

That rebuilding begins with a simple truth: the player is not broken. Colour blindness is not stupidity, laziness, weak mentality or low football intelligence. It is not proof that the player cannot succeed. It does not explain every mistake, and it does not remove the need to train, listen, improve and compete. But it may explain why some football situations felt more confusing than they looked from the outside.

That distinction matters. A player who believes every mistake means they are not good enough can become trapped. A player who learns to separate the football problem from the picture problem has a way forward. The football problem can be trained. The picture problem can be managed. The emotional wound can be understood. None of that makes the game easy, but it makes the player less alone inside it.

Many colour-blind players will have already built their own survival strategies without knowing it. They may use numbers, socks, shorts, voices, positions, movement patterns, body shape or direction of attack. They may wait for teammates to call. They may pass to the safest visible option. They may avoid crowded areas where identification is harder. They may scan longer before receiving. Some of those strategies help. Some may have become too cautious. The goal is not to throw them away, but to make them deliberate.

A deliberate cue is different from a desperate check. A desperate check happens under pressure, when the ball is arriving and the player is already unsure. A deliberate cue is planned before the pressure comes. The player knows what to look for. They know which colours are risky. They know whether socks, shorts, numbers or voices are more reliable. They know what to do if the picture becomes unclear. That plan reduces panic.

The first step is to learn the player’s own colour map. Not every colour-blind player struggles in exactly the same way. One player may find red and green difficult. Another may struggle with orange on grass. Another may confuse blue and purple. Another may find dark kits difficult under floodlights. Another may be affected more by poor light, mud, glare or distance. Some players may also have eyesight issues, blurred vision or light sensitivity that make the picture harder. The player should not guess alone. Colour vision and eyesight should be checked properly by qualified eye-care professionals, but the player can also learn from football experience.

After matches or training, the player can ask: which cues were clear today? Which ones were not? Did I trust shirts? Did socks help? Did numbers help? Was the goalkeeper kit confusing? Did the referee blend in? Were cones hard to find? Did the light make colours worse? Did I hesitate because I did not know what to do, or because I was not sure who was who? These questions turn shame into information.

The player also needs a trusted adult. That might be a parent, coach, teacher, academy staff member, older sibling or teammate. The conversation does not need to be dramatic. The player can say, “Some colours are harder for me to separate quickly. I am not asking for easier football, but numbers, names and clearer cues help me play faster.” That sentence gives the adult something practical. It says the player still wants standards. It also says the player needs clearer information.

Not every player will want teammates to know. That choice should be respected where possible. Some players may be comfortable explaining it openly. Others may prefer the coach to adjust sessions quietly. A young child may need a parent to help. A teenager may want control over who is told. Trust is easier to rebuild when the player does not feel exposed.

The next step is to build match-day anchors. Anchors are reliable cues the player can return to when colour becomes uncertain. A shirt may be one cue, but it should not be the only one. The player can learn to check socks and shorts before kick-off. They can notice whether their team has a unique number style, stripe, sleeve, collar, pattern or sponsor mark. They can use the direction of attack. They can listen for early names. They can identify teammates by body shape, movement habits or positions. They can agree with close teammates to call earlier in crowded moments.

Anchors should be simple enough to use under pressure. Too many cues can create the paradox of choice again. The player does not need a list of twelve things to check while being pressed. They need a small hierarchy. First cue: shirt if clear. Second cue: socks or shorts. Third cue: number or voice. Emergency cue: bounce pass, protect, call, reset. The hierarchy should be practised until it becomes familiar.

Training should support that. A coach can help by designing drills where the player uses non-colour cues on purpose. Numbered bibs. Named zones. Directional gates. Voice-before-pass rules. Small-sided games where players must call names before receiving. Passing patterns where the target is described by position rather than colour. These drills should not be framed as remedial. They are football intelligence drills. They improve communication and awareness for everyone.

The player also needs a reset after mistakes. Colour-blind players who have been embarrassed before may let one wrong pass ruin the next ten minutes. The mistake triggers old shame. The body tightens. The mind starts checking harder. The player becomes more cautious, which can create more mistakes. A reset line gives the player a way back.

A strong reset line might be: “Find the next clear cue.” Another might be: “One mistake, next picture.” Another might be: “Check, call, play.” The exact words matter less than the function. The reset should stop the mistake from becoming identity. It should move the player from shame into action.

Coaches can support this by separating the idea from the cue. If the player attempted a good pass but misidentified the shirt, the coach can say, “Good idea, wrong cue. Let’s fix the cue.” That sentence is powerful because it protects football intelligence while still correcting the outcome. It tells the player that the idea had value and that the problem can be improved. The player learns not to throw away brave football because one visual cue failed.

Players should also learn when to advocate for themselves. Self-advocacy does not mean complaining. It means giving useful information. “Those bibs are hard for me to separate.” “Numbers help me.” “Can you say the cone by position as well as colour?” “The goalkeeper kit is close to ours.” “I can play quicker if people call names early.” These are not excuses. They are performance details. Good athletes learn what helps them perform.

This may feel uncomfortable at first. Some players fear being treated differently. Some worry that coaches will think they are difficult. Some worry teammates will laugh. That fear is real, especially if the player has already been misunderstood. But silence has a cost too. If nobody knows the cue is unclear, the player may keep being judged inside the same confusion. The goal is not to tell everyone. The goal is to tell the right person in the right way.

Parents can help players practise the language. At home, they can role-play a short conversation with a coach. They can help the child write down difficult colours or situations. They can remind the child that asking for clearer cues is not asking for football to be easier. They can also keep the emotional frame steady. The message should be: “This helps us understand you better. It does not make you less of a player.”

For older players, trust may also require grieving what was missed. If colour blindness is discovered late, the player may feel angry. They may think about trials, coaches, matches, money, missed chances and old labels. They may wonder whether they could have adapted earlier. That grief should not be dismissed with “at least you know now.” Knowing now is useful, but it does not erase what came before. The player may need space to feel the regret before turning it into action.

At the same time, the player should not let late discovery become a new prison. The past may explain some things, but it should not take ownership of the future. The player can still improve. They can still enjoy football. They can still build better habits. They can still play at a level that gives meaning, identity and pride. The dream may change, but the player does not have to stay trapped in the old shame.

Trust returns through repeated evidence. The player needs to experience clear cues working. They need matches where they use numbers and make good decisions. They need teammates who call early. They need coaches who correct the football without humiliating the player. They need parents who notice effort and patterns rather than only outcomes. They need to feel the game becoming playable again.

This rebuilding may also involve eyesight support. If the player has poor eyesight, blurred vision, headaches or light sensitivity as well as colour blindness, that should be handled with professional guidance. Glasses may help some players, but can cause practical problems for others in football, such as movement, discomfort, falling off or limited confidence in contact. Sports goggles may help in some cases, but some players may find they affect peripheral vision. Contact lenses may be a practical option for some players, but only with proper advice. The key message is not one solution for everyone. The key message is: check eyesight and colour vision separately, and find what works safely for the player.

Players also need to understand that colour blindness is not something to be defeated by effort. Trying harder will not make red and green separate like they do for someone else. Concentrating more will not always make an orange cone stand out on grass. Bravery will not fix a kit clash. Effort matters in football, but effort should be aimed at the right problem. The player can work hard on scanning, communication, cue systems, positioning, decision-making and confidence. They should not waste years trying to force their eyes to become someone else’s.

That realisation can be freeing. The player can stop asking, “What is wrong with me?” and start asking, “What information do I need?” They can stop treating every mistake as proof of failure and start studying the conditions around the mistake. They can stop hiding the whole issue from themselves and start building a practical game.

Finding trust again does not mean the player will never make a colour-related mistake. They might. Football is fast and imperfect. Even with preparation, some situations will be difficult. The goal is not perfection. The goal is resilience. The player learns to recover quicker, communicate earlier, use better cues and avoid turning one uncertain moment into a whole identity.

For coaches and parents, the most important thing is to support that resilience without making the player feel fragile. Do not wrap the player in fear. Do not treat colour blindness as the end of ambition. Do not let every conversation become about what they cannot see. Balance matters. The player needs honesty and tools, but also normal football expectations. Praise courage. Coach technique. Demand communication. Celebrate improvement. Hold standards after the picture is fair.

That is how trust becomes strong. Not by pretending the issue does not exist, and not by letting the issue define the player. Trust grows when the player knows what is hard, knows what helps, and knows the adults around them will judge the football more fairly because the picture has been considered.

Seeing clearly is the beginning. Playing freely is the goal. A player who has spent years checking, worrying and shrinking may not become free immediately. But with the right cues, the right support and the right language, they can begin to play from confidence instead of fear.

The next clear cue is where freedom starts.

Chapter 12 — Building the Colour-Safe Game

One good coach can help one child. A better system can protect the next hundred.

That is where this book has to move before it ends. It cannot stay only with one player, one parent, one coach or one match. Colour blindness in football is personal, but the solution cannot depend only on personal luck. A child should not be protected only if they happen to have a parent who notices, a coach who understands, a teacher who has experience, or a club that has already thought about colour vision. The game itself has to become better designed.

A colour-safe game is not a perfect game. It is not a game where every colour problem disappears, every kit is ideal, every club has unlimited money, and every coach becomes a specialist in vision. That is not realistic. Football is messy. Grassroots clubs have limited budgets. Schools use whatever equipment is available. Coaches are busy. Weather changes. Kits clash. Matches are rushed. Children arrive late. Parents forget things. Referees do their best. No system can remove every problem.

But a better system can remove many avoidable ones.

The first step is to understand that colour-safe football is not special treatment. It is good football design. The aim is not to give one player an advantage. The aim is to make important information clear enough for players to use. That helps colour-blind players, but it also helps younger players, tired players, nervous players, new players, players under pressure, players who missed the first instruction and players still learning how to communicate. Clarity improves the session for everyone.

A club that takes this seriously does not wait for a child to be embarrassed. It builds clarity into normal practice. It buys bibs with stronger contrast. It avoids combinations that are likely to cause confusion. It chooses cones that stand out against the surface. It encourages coaches to use names, numbers, positions and directions as well as colour. It checks kits before match day where possible. It teaches coaches to ask, “What did you see?” before turning a mistake into a label. It gives parents a private way to raise concerns. It treats colour vision as part of football information, not as an awkward side issue.

Schools can do the same. A PE lesson should not depend on colour alone if colour carries important meaning. If children are sent to coloured cones, the cones can be described by position too. If teams are divided by bibs, teachers can use clear contrast and add names or numbers. If maps, diagrams or sports instructions use colour, they can include labels, patterns, shapes or text. These changes do not make the lesson weaker. They make it clearer.

Academies and development programmes have an even greater responsibility because their judgements can shape a child’s pathway. If a trial uses unclear kits, colour-coded groups, fast tactical instructions and no alternative cues, some players may be judged inside avoidable confusion. That does not mean every unsuccessful player was treated unfairly. It means high-stakes environments should take visual clarity seriously. If selection matters, the information should be clear enough for the selection to mean something.

Referees, organisers and tournament staff also have a role. Match-day kit clashes are not only a television issue. They happen at youth and grassroots level too. Two teams may look different standing still but become difficult when players move, sweat, get muddy or play under poor light. Goalkeeper shirts and referee shirts can add to the confusion. A simple greyscale check, a contrast check, a spare bib option or a willingness to change tops where possible can prevent avoidable problems before the match begins.

The same thinking applies to equipment. Clubs often buy what is cheap, available or familiar. That is understandable, but colour clarity should be part of equipment decisions. Bib packs should not only contain bright colours; they should contain combinations that separate strongly. Cones should be chosen for the surfaces where they will actually be used. Balls should be visible against grass, mud, indoor floors or artificial surfaces. Tactical boards should allow numbers, names or initials, not only coloured dots. If a club has a limited budget, it can still make better choices with the money it already spends.

A colour-safe club does not need to become complicated. It can start with a small number of practical habits. Check whether instructions rely only on colour. Check whether bibs separate in movement. Check whether cones stand out from the surface. Check whether kits still separate in greyscale. Check whether coaches know how to respond privately if a parent raises colour vision. Check whether players are being exposed publicly or supported with dignity. These habits are simple, but they change the culture.

Culture matters because children learn what is safe to say. If a club jokes about colour blindness, players hide. If a coach dismisses a parent by saying, “He knows his colours,” the conversation closes. If a team treats clearer cues as embarrassing, the child becomes the problem. But if a club treats clear communication as normal football behaviour, the issue becomes easier to handle. The player does not have to carry the weight alone.

The language should be performance-based. Instead of saying, “We are changing everything for one player,” say, “We are improving communication and clarity for everyone.” Instead of saying, “He can’t see colours,” say privately, “Some colours are harder for him to separate quickly, so numbers and names help him play faster.” Instead of saying, “This is an inclusion issue only,” say, “This is also performance design.” That language keeps standards high and protects dignity.

Sponsors can help because this issue is practical. A local business, optician, sports brand, charity, football foundation or community organisation can support real changes: free books, parent guides, coach checklists, player reset cards, colour-safe equipment packs, better bibs, clearer cones, high-contrast balls, school PE resources, digital games and awareness workshops. Sponsorship should not be framed only as a logo on a page. It can be framed as helping children be seen fairly.

A sponsor who funds colour-safe resources is not buying attention alone. They are helping a parent ask a better question before confidence breaks. They are helping a coach change a session before a player is misjudged. They are helping a club avoid avoidable kit confusion. They are helping a child feel that the problem is not stupidity, laziness or weakness. That is meaningful community impact.

Digital tools can also extend the book beyond the page. Online games, training resources, checklists, QR codes, downloadable guides and a future book companion can help players practise cues beyond colour. A game can train a player to use numbers, positions, direction and kit features. A coach resource can show how kit clashes appear in greyscale. A parent checklist can help families track patterns. A club policy starter can turn awareness into action. The printed book should open the door; the resources should help people walk through it.

Do not make one player the poster child without consent. Do not make public announcements that embarrass a young person. Build systems that help quietly. A colour-safe game is not only about visibility on the pitch. It is also about emotional safety off it.

This is especially important because some players may not want to be known as colour blind. They may be chasing selection. They may fear being treated as a risk. They may worry teammates will laugh. They may not want parents to make a fuss. They may still be processing what the diagnosis means. Clubs should create routes for private disclosure and support. The player’s dignity should not depend on whether adults are excited about awareness.

A good system also recognises that colour blindness may exist alongside other visual issues. Some players may have poor eyesight, blurred vision, headaches, light sensitivity or difficulty with glare. These should be checked properly by qualified professionals. Clubs and coaches should not guess, diagnose or prescribe solutions. They should encourage proper testing and then use practical information to support the player safely. Eyesight and colour vision are related to how the game is seen, but they are not the same question.

Building the colour-safe game also means training coaches to think in layers. The immediate layer is the session: bibs, cones, instructions, teams, zones and communication. The match-day layer is kits, goalkeeper shirts, referee colours, light, mud and tactical cues. The club layer is equipment buying, coach education, parent communication, privacy and policy. The community layer is schools, sponsors, resources, awareness and access. Each layer matters because the child moves through all of them.

The work can begin with thirty days. In the first week, a club can audit its bibs, cones, balls and tactical boards. In the second week, coaches can start using colour-plus cues: colour plus number, colour plus direction, colour plus role, colour plus name. In the third week, the club can introduce a private parent/player route for concerns and share basic guidance about colour vision testing. In the fourth week, the club can review match-day kit checks, create a simple policy and connect families to resources.

Build a school session where colour is not the only instruction. Build an academy trial where visual clarity is part of fairness. Build a sponsor campaign that puts books and tools into the hands of people who need them. Build digital resources that help players practise what the book teaches.

The colour-safe game is not about making football soft. It is about making football visible enough for the real game to happen.

This checklist is not a diagnosis tool. It is a pattern-noticing tool.

A child can show one or two of these signs and not be colour blind. A child can be colour blind and not show every sign. Avoids conversations about colour mistakes.

At School

Colour vision difficulties may also appear in schoolwork, especially when lessons rely on colour-coded information.

Struggles with colour-coded maps, graphs, diagrams or charts.

Finds science diagrams, geography maps or maths graphs harder when colour carries the meaning.

Has difficulty with coloured pens, worksheets or labels.

Copies another pupil’s colour choices.

Avoids colouring tasks or becomes frustrated by them.

Avoids answering when colour is involved.

Gets embarrassed if other children notice a colour mistake.

Says the work is confusing but cannot explain why.

Finds PE cones, bibs or colour-coded teams difficult to follow.

Struggles to separate bib colours quickly.

Asks who is on which team more often than other players.

Struggles with red, orange or green cones on grass.

Finds some cone colours harder on mud, artificial surfaces or indoor floors.

Looks confused when a coach says “go to the red cone,” “blue team press,” or “green group rotate.”

Needs extra time to begin a colour-coded drill.

Watches what other players do before acting.

Performs better when drills use names, numbers, positions or directions instead of colour alone.

Seems sharp in some drills but lost in others where colour is more important.

Plays more confidently when bibs are clearly different.

Hesitates when bibs are similar or dark against dark.

Uses numbers, socks, shorts, voices or positions more than shirt colour.

Appears not to listen when the real issue may be unclear colour information.

Passes to the wrong team when kits are similar.

Hesitates before passing in certain kit conditions.

Looks confident in one match and unsure in another without an obvious reason.

Plays better when teams are visually clear.

Struggles more under floodlights, low sun, shadow, rain or muddy conditions.

Takes extra touches before passing.

Holds the ball too long while checking options.

Chooses safer passes when forward passes are available.

Avoids receiving the ball in crowded areas.

Seems worried before receiving.

Says, “I wasn’t sure.”

If the child plays freely when kits are clear but becomes cautious when colours are close, that is worth noticing. If the child repeatedly struggles with cones on grass, that is worth noticing. If the child worries about passing to the wrong player, that is worth noticing.

Noticing is not diagnosing. It is the first step towards asking better questions.

“This might explain some things. It does not mean you cannot play football. It means we can understand the picture better and find what helps.”

“We are looking into possible colour vision issues. We are not asking you to lower standards, but we have noticed that certain bibs, kits or cones may be harder for my child to separate quickly. Names, numbers, positions and clearer contrast seem to help.”

Your child does not need blame. They need understanding.

They do not need to be publicly exposed. They need dignity.

They do not need every mistake explained away. They need the right mistake coached.

They do not need adults to panic. They need adults to notice patterns early enough to help.

The goal is not to prove colour blindness from the touchline. The goal is to stop a child being judged inside a picture nobody has checked.

The date of the match, training session or school issue.

The colours of both teams’ kits.

The colour of the bibs used in training.

The colour of the cones or markers.

The pitch surface: grass, mud, artificial turf, indoor floor or another surface.

The light conditions: bright sun, low sun, shade, rain, floodlights or indoor lighting.

Whether the child hesitated, passed to the wrong team, missed a cone, asked who was on which team, or seemed worried.

What the child said afterwards.

Whether the child performed better when colours were clearer.

Whether similar problems appear at home or school.

A single mistake is not enough to conclude anything. A repeated pattern is more useful.

Step 2 — Speak to Your Child Calmly

Choose a quiet time, not immediately after a painful mistake if emotions are high. The goal is not to interrogate the child. The goal is to help them feel safe enough to describe the game from the inside.

A useful opening might be:

“I have noticed that some football situations seem harder when the kits, bibs or cones are close in colour. I am not angry and I am not saying you did anything wrong. I just want to understand what you saw.”

Then ask simple questions:

Were the kits easy to tell apart?

Did any colours look similar?

Could you see the cones quickly?

Were you worried about passing to the wrong player?

Did numbers, socks, shorts or voices help?

Does this happen in school or at home too?

Would you like help asking about it?

The child may not answer clearly at first. That is normal. They may not know. They may feel embarrassed. They may worry that it sounds like an excuse. Keep the conversation calm and practical.

Step 3 — Protect the Child’s Dignity

Do not announce the issue publicly. Do not tell the whole team without the child’s consent. Do not speak about the child as if they are broken. Do not turn one possible visual issue into the child’s whole identity.

A useful message for the child is:

“This does not mean you cannot play football. It means we may need to understand what information helps you play your best.”

The child needs to know that they are still a footballer. They still need to train, listen, improve and take responsibility. But they should not be forced to carry shame for a picture nobody has checked.

Step 4 — Book Proper Eye and Colour Vision Checks

If you are concerned, speak to a qualified eye-care professional. Ask specifically about colour vision testing. A standard eyesight check and a colour vision test are not always the same thing.

Mention any additional concerns, including:

Blurred vision.

Headaches.

Eye strain.

Light sensitivity.

Difficulty with glare.

Trouble seeing the board at school.

Difficulty tracking the ball.

Problems with glasses during sport.

Any previous eye-care history.

If the child already wears glasses or contact lenses, still ask about colour vision separately if the pattern suggests it. Sharp eyesight and reliable colour separation are not the same thing.

Do not guess from football alone. Use football as a reason to ask better professional questions.

Step 5 — Speak to the Coach Privately

Once you have a concern or a confirmed colour vision issue, speak to the coach privately. Keep the tone practical. The aim is not to accuse, blame or demand special treatment. The aim is to help the coach understand what may help the child play quicker and with more confidence.

A useful parent-to-coach message:

“We are looking into possible colour vision issues. We are not asking for lower standards, but we have noticed that certain bibs, kits or cones may be harder for my child to separate quickly. Names, numbers, positions and stronger contrast seem to help. Could we keep an eye on whether some setups make the game harder to read?”

If colour blindness has been confirmed, you might say:

“My child has colour vision deficiency. They can still play and still need normal football standards, but some colours are harder to separate quickly. It helps when instructions use names, numbers, positions or directions as well as colour.”

Ask the child first how much they are comfortable sharing, especially if they are older.

Step 6 — Help Build Reliable Football Cues

Help the child identify what works. Do not make them obsess over colour. Give them a simple plan.

Possible cues include:

Shirt numbers.

Player names.

Voices.

Socks.

Shorts.

Direction of attack.

Positions.

Body shape.

Movement patterns.

Strong contrast.

Pre-match team checks.

Early teammate calls.

A reset action after mistakes.

Before a match, ask:

“What cues look clearest today?”

After a match, ask:

“What helped you most today?”

This teaches the child to study the game practically, not shamefully.

Step 7 — Watch for Worry

A child may not only struggle with colours. They may worry about colours all game. That worry can waste energy and reduce focus.

Watch for signs such as:

Fear of passing to the wrong player.

Asking repeatedly who is on which team.

Playing safe after a wrong-team pass.

Avoiding the ball.

Looking tense before receiving.

Becoming quiet after mistakes.

Saying they are stupid, slow or not good enough.

Seeming mentally tired after visually confusing games.

If the child is worrying, reassure them that this is not weakness. It may be the result of trying to play inside uncertainty. Then help them build a plan with trusted cues.

Step 8 — Keep Football Standards, but Make the Test Fair

The goal is not to remove challenge. The child should still be coached properly. They should still learn to scan, communicate, pass, defend, compete and make decisions. But if the visual setup is unclear, the child may be judged before the football action is fairly understood.

Parents should avoid two extremes.

The first extreme is ignoring the pattern and telling the child to “just concentrate.”

The second extreme is blaming every mistake on colour blindness.

The better path is balanced:

“Let’s make the picture clearer, then coach the football.”

Step 9 — Review Progress

After changes are made, watch whether anything improves.

Notice:

Does the child play quicker when teams are easier to separate?

Do they ask for the ball more?

Do they seem less worried?

Do they pass with more confidence when names and numbers are used?

Do they struggle less when cones are clearer?

Do they recover faster after mistakes?

Does the coach understand the issue better?

Does the child feel safer talking about it?

Improvement does not have to be perfect to matter. Even small improvements can protect confidence.

Step 10 — Use Regret Carefully

If the issue is discovered late, regret may appear. The player may wonder what would have happened if they had known earlier. The parent may feel guilty for not spotting it sooner. Handle that gently.

Do not tell the child that colour blindness ruined everything. Do not tell yourself you failed as a parent. Football careers are shaped by many things, and nobody can rewrite the past with one explanation.

A better message is:

“We know more now. We can use that knowledge from here.”

The purpose of discovering the issue is not to live in regret. It is to help the child now, and to help the next child earlier.

Parent Next Steps Summary

Notice patterns calmly over time.

Ask your child what they saw, not only why they made the mistake.

Protect your child’s dignity and privacy.

Ask a qualified eye-care professional specifically about colour vision testing if you have concerns.

Remember that eyesight and colour vision are not the same thing.

Speak to the coach privately and practically.

Help your child build trusted football cues.

Watch for worry, decision fatigue and loss of confidence.

Use regret as a reason to act, not as a reason to blame yourself or your child.

Appendix D — Parent-to-Coach Script

This script is designed to help parents speak to a coach calmly, privately and practically.

The aim is not to blame the coach. The aim is not to demand special treatment. The aim is not to excuse every mistake. The aim is to share useful information that may help the coach understand the player better, make the football picture clearer, and judge the player more fairly.

Many have never been taught to think about colour vision in football. If the conversation begins with accusation, the coach may become defensive. “Hi Coach, could I have a quick private word when you have a moment? It is about something we have noticed that may affect how my child sees certain colours in football. It is not a complaint and we are not asking for lower standards. I just want to share it so we can support them properly.”

using names or numbers as well as colours;

Appendix E — Coach Session Clarity Checklist

This checklist is for coaches before, during and after training.

It is not designed to make sessions soft. It is designed to make sessions clearer, faster and fairer. A coach can only judge the football properly when the player can understand the information the session is built on.

Use this checklist as a quick habit. It should not take long. Many of the best changes take seconds.

Before the Session

Before the players arrive, look at the setup as if someone in the group may struggle with colour. Do not wait until a child tells you they are colour blind. Many children will not know. Some will be embarrassed. Some will stay silent. Build clarity into the session from the start.

Ask:

Can the teams be identified without relying on colour alone?

Are the bib colours clearly different in movement, not just in your hand?

Are the bibs strong enough in contrast?

Are you using risky combinations such as red/green, red/orange, green/yellow, blue/purple or dark/dark?

If bibs are not ideal, can you add numbers, names, directions or roles?

Are the cones easy to see against the surface?

Are red or orange cones being used on grass as the only important cue?

Are any markers difficult to see in shade, mud, low sun or floodlights?

Can each cone or zone be described by position, number or shape as well as colour?

Does the drill still make sense if a player cannot use the colour cue quickly?

Are tactical board magnets labelled with numbers, initials or positions?

Are instructions simple enough to follow without colour-only language?

Bib Check

Bibs are one of the most common hidden colour problems in training. Do not assume different colour names mean clear separation.

Better bib habits include:

Use strong contrast whenever possible.

Avoid red versus green where possible.

Avoid red versus orange where possible.

Avoid green versus yellow where possible.

Avoid blue versus purple where possible.

Avoid dark bibs over dark clothing where possible.

Consider whether the bibs separate when players move quickly.

Consider whether rain, mud, shade or indoor lighting will reduce contrast.

Add non-colour cues when bib choices are limited.

Use team names if colours are difficult.

Use direction of attack as a cue.

Use numbers or roles where possible.

A useful coach sentence:

“You are the team attacking the clubhouse. You are the team attacking the car park.”

That is clearer than colour alone.

Cone and Marker Check

Cones are often used as if every player sees them the same way. They may not.

Before the drill, ask:

Does this cone stand out from the surface?

Would it still be clear from distance?

Would it still be clear while the player is dribbling?

Would it still be clear in peripheral vision?

Does mud, shadow or low sun make it harder?

Is the cone colour the only instruction?

Could the cone be numbered?

Could the cone be described by position?

Could the cone be replaced with a stronger contrast marker?

Use second cues:

“The cone nearest the goal.”

“Cone number two.”

“The tall cone.”

“The left gate.”

“The square nearest me.”

“The gate beside the touchline.”

This helps every player, not just colour-blind players.

Instruction Check

Coaches often use colour because it is quick. But quick for the coach may not be quick for every player.

Avoid relying only on instructions like:

“Red team press.”

“Blue group rotate.”

“Go to the orange cone.”

“Defend the green gate.”

“Stand on the red line.”

“Pass to yellow.”

Stronger versions include:

“Red team, attacking the clubhouse, press.”

“Blue group, group one, rotate.”

“Go to the orange cone nearest the goal.”

“Defend the left-hand gate.”

“Stand on the line nearest the halfway mark.”

“Pass to Jamie’s team.”

“Number four, switch.”

“Wide players rotate.”

“Front three press.”

Use colour plus another cue.

Colour plus number.

Colour plus name.

Colour plus position.

Colour plus direction.

Colour plus shape.

Colour plus role.

Watch for players who may be struggling with the picture rather than the football.

Possible signs include:

Asking who is on which team.

Watching others before acting.

Running to the wrong cone.

Passing to the wrong team.

Hesitating before simple passes.

Looking better when teams are clearer.

Playing safe when bibs are similar.

Taking extra touches before passing.

Avoiding receiving in crowded areas.

Looking nervous after a wrong-team pass.

Becoming quiet after being corrected.

Repeatedly struggling with one colour-coded drill but not others.

Do not assume the answer too quickly. Ask privately where possible:

“What did you see?”

“Were the bibs clear?”

“Could you see the cone quickly?”

“Were you unsure who was on your team?”

“What cue were you using?”

“Would numbers or names help?”

These questions are not soft. They help you coach the correct problem.

If one player may be struggling with colour, do not announce it publicly.

Do not say:

“We are changing this because he cannot see colours.”

Do not joke about it.

Do not make the player the reason for the whole group changing.

Use neutral language:

“We are making the setup clearer.”

“We are using names and numbers today.”

“We are improving communication.”

“We are making the drill faster to understand.”

“We are checking the picture before we play.”

The player should not have to carry the issue publicly for the group to benefit from clearer coaching.

When a mistake happens, the coach should still coach. But first, ask whether the player had clear information.

Instead of:

“What are you doing?”

Try:

“What did you see?”

Instead of:

“You have to see that pass.”

Try:

“What cue were you using?”

Instead of:

“You are too slow.”

Try:

“Did you need to check who was on your team?”

Instead of:

“You are not listening.”

Try:

“Was that cone easy to find?”

If the player simply made a poor football decision, coach the decision. If the visual cue was unclear, fix the cue first. Then coach the football.

passing;

receiving;

movement;

communication;

pressure;

defending;

attacking;

decision-making;

bravery;

tactical understanding.

whether a child can separate red from green at speed;

whether an orange cone stands out on grass;

whether two dark bib colours separate in shade;

whether a tactical board can be understood through colour alone;

whether a child is brave enough to admit they cannot see the cue.

Appendix F — Coach Match-Day Clarity Checklist

Match day is where unclear information can become expensive.

In training, a confusing bib or cone may slow a drill down. In a match, a confusing kit or unclear cue can change a result, damage confidence, affect selection, and become part of how a player is judged. A wrong-team pass in a competitive moment is not forgotten as quickly as a training mistake. A player who hesitates under pressure may carry that feeling into the next game. A coach who loses trust may make different decisions the following week.

That is why match-day clarity matters.

This checklist is for coaches, managers, referees, club volunteers and tournament organisers. It is not designed to create perfect conditions. It is designed to reduce avoidable confusion before the match starts.

Before Kick-Off

Look at both teams together, not separately.

A kit can look clear when one team is warming up alone, then become confusing when both teams are moving in the same space. Do not judge the kits only from close distance. Step back. Look from the touchline. Look from behind the players. Look while they are moving. Think about what the game will look like from a player’s angle, not only from an adult’s stationary view.

Ask:

Do the two teams separate clearly at speed?

Do the shirts separate clearly?

Do the shorts separate clearly?

Do the socks separate clearly?

Does the overall moving outline of each team look different enough?

Would the teams still separate if the shirts were muddy?

Would the teams still separate from the side or back?

Would the teams still separate in peripheral vision?

Would the teams still separate under low sun, floodlights, shade or rain?

Could a colour-blind player use something other than shirt colour to tell teams apart?

Shirt, Shorts and Socks Check

Do not check shirts only.

Players often see each other from the side, from behind, or through quick glimpses in movement. Socks and shorts can become important cues, especially when shirts are too similar or partly hidden.

Check:

Are both teams wearing similar shirt colours?

Are both teams wearing similar short colours?

Are both teams wearing similar sock colours?

If shirts are close, do shorts or socks give a strong second cue?

If socks are muddy, will the second cue disappear?

A player does not need to trust colour alone.

That is the point of match-day anchors. An anchor is a cue the player can use when colour is unclear, slow or unreliable. It helps the player spend less energy checking and more energy playing.

Match-day anchors are not excuses. They are performance tools.

Every player uses cues. Players read body shape, pressure, space, voice, timing, movement and direction. A colour-blind player may need to make those cues more deliberate because shirt colour, bib colour or cone colour may not always be fast enough. The goal is not to make football easier. The goal is to make the information clearer so the player can play with more trust.

Before the Match

Before the game starts, take a quiet moment to study the picture. Do not wait until the ball is already moving.

Ask yourself:

Are the shorts clear?

Are the socks clear?

Are the numbers clear?

Does the goalkeeper kit clash with either team?

Does the referee shirt look close to either team?

Will mud make the socks or shorts harder to use?

Will low sun, shade, rain or floodlights make the colours harder?

A cue hierarchy means knowing what to check first, second and third.

This prevents the player from checking everything at once. Too many cues can create overthinking. The player does not need twelve things in their head when the ball arrives. They need a simple order.

Second cue: socks or shorts, if shirts are close.

Third cue: number, voice or position.

The hierarchy should be simple enough to remember under pressure. The player can build it before kick-off with a parent, coach or trusted teammate.

A reset script is what a player uses when the mistake has happened, the worry has arrived, or the picture suddenly feels unclear.

It is not magic. It will not remove pressure. It will not stop every wrong pass, every hesitation or every difficult match. But it gives the player something to do before shame takes over. That matters, because one colour-related mistake can quickly become more than one mistake. The player passes to the wrong shirt, hears the shout, feels embarrassed, starts checking harder, plays safer, avoids the next pass and slowly disappears from the game.

The reset script is designed to interrupt that cycle.

The aim is simple: do not let one unclear picture become the whole match.

The Main Reset Line

Use this line after a mistake or when doubt starts rising:

That is the main reset.

Not, “I am useless.”

Not, “I always do this.”

Not, “Everyone thinks I am bad.”

Not, “Do not mess up again.”

Just:

Find the next clear cue.

The next clear cue might be a number, a voice, a sock colour, a short colour, a teammate’s movement, the direction of attack, a safe pass, the goalkeeper, the touchline or a simple call. The point is to move the mind away from shame and back towards information.

Reset After a Wrong-Team Pass

A wrong-team pass can hurt because it happens publicly. The player may feel exposed. The mistake may bring back memories of previous mistakes. The urge may be to hide, blame themselves or stop asking for the ball.

Use this reset:

Step 1: Breathe.

One breath is enough to stop the mistake becoming panic.

Step 2: Name the problem.

Was it the football decision, the visual cue, or both?

Step 3: Find the next clear cue.

Switch to the strongest cue available.

Step 4: Make one simple football action.

Press, recover, call, mark, show for a pass or play the next safe action.

Do not solve the whole problem during the match.

Appendix I — Greyscale Phone Test

A greyscale phone test is a simple way to check whether kits, bibs, cones or equipment may be too close in contrast.

It is not a medical test. It is not a perfect scientific measure. It does not replace official kit guidance, professional accessibility checks or proper colour vision advice. But it is a useful practical habit because it helps coaches, parents, players and clubs see something they might otherwise miss.

Sometimes two colours look different to a person with typical colour vision, but when the colour is removed, the lightness and darkness are very similar. If two kits look almost the same in greyscale, they may be harder for some colour-blind players to separate quickly during a match. The same can happen with bibs, cones, goalkeeper shirts and referee shirts.

The greyscale test is not about proving a kit clash. It is about spotting a possible warning sign before the warning sign becomes a wrong pass.

Why Greyscale Helps

Colour is not the only thing that helps players separate information. Contrast matters too.

If one team is wearing a very light kit and the other team is wearing a very dark kit, the teams may be easier to separate even for players who struggle with certain colours. If two teams are wearing different colours but those colours have similar lightness, they may become harder to separate when the game moves quickly.

For example, two kits may have different colour names but still look similar in tone. A red kit and a green kit may seem different to many people, but for some colour-blind players they may not separate well. An orange bib and a green surface may seem bright to a coach, but may not stand out reliably for every child. A goalkeeper shirt may look different in colour but too close in brightness to an outfield kit.

Greyscale removes the colour and lets you focus on contrast. If the objects still separate clearly in black and white, that is a good sign. If they collapse into similar shades, there may be a problem.

How to Do the Greyscale Phone Test

Use a phone camera before a match or training session.

Step 1: Put both teams, bib groups or equipment colours in the same photo where possible.

Step 2: Stand far enough back to see the players or equipment the way they will appear during the game.

Step 3: Take a photo in the real conditions: same pitch, same light, same surface, same weather if possible.

Step 4: Convert the photo to black and white or greyscale using the phone’s photo editing tools.

Step 5: Look at the photo without thinking about the colour names.

Step 6: Ask: do the teams or objects still separate clearly?

Step 7: If they look too similar, add another cue or change the setup where possible.

This test is most useful when checking the full picture, not isolated items. A shirt may look fine alone, but the clash may appear when shirts, shorts, socks, goalkeeper kits, referee shirts, grass, mud and shadow are all present together.

What to Check

Use the greyscale test for:

home kit against away kit;

shirts, shorts and socks together;

goalkeeper kit against both teams;

referee shirt against both teams and goalkeepers;

bib colour combinations;

cones against grass, mud, indoor floors or artificial turf;

training zones;

balls against the playing surface;

tactical board magnets;

coloured lines or markers.

The question is always the same:

If colour disappeared, would the important information still be clear enough?

Kit Check

When checking kits, do not photograph shirts only. Football is not played with shirts floating in the air. Players see full moving bodies.

Check:

shirt contrast;

shorts contrast;

socks contrast;

number contrast;

goalkeeper contrast;

referee contrast;

mud risk;

shadow risk;

whether the teams separate from the side and back.

A kit that passes from the front may still be difficult from behind. A kit that passes before kick-off may become difficult after mud covers socks. A kit that passes in bright light may become difficult under floodlights or low sun.

The greyscale test cannot predict every problem, but it can help adults see whether the overall picture is risky.

Bib Check

Bibs often create problems because coaches choose colours quickly from whatever is available.

Use the greyscale test to check bib combinations before the session begins. If two bib colours look similar in greyscale, they may not separate quickly enough for some players. This is especially important in small-sided games where players are moving quickly, turning, pressing and making decisions under pressure.

If bib colours are limited, add non-colour cues:

one team attacks one direction;

teams are named rather than colour-coded;

players call names before passes;

bibs are numbered if possible;

one team wears bibs and one team does not;

teams are described by starting position;

coaches use “Jamie’s team” or “left-side team” instead of colour only.

Cone Check

Cones should be checked against the surface where they are being used.

A cone that looks bright in the coach’s hand may become weaker on grass, mud, artificial turf or indoor flooring. Red and orange cones can be especially risky on grass for some players, depending on the type of colour vision difficulty and the surface conditions.

Use the greyscale test by placing the cone on the actual surface, then taking a photo from the distance and angle players will see it. If the cone does not stand out in greyscale, do not rely on colour alone.

Add second cues:

cone numbers;

cone positions;

tall cones;

different shapes;

gates described by left, right, near or far;

coach standing near the target;

verbal reminders;

white or blue markers where they stand out better.

The aim is not to remove cones. The aim is to make sure the cone is information, not confusion.

Tactical Board Check

A tactical board can become a colour test if it relies only on coloured magnets or lines.

Use the greyscale test on the board if needed. If the magnets become too similar, add:

player numbers;

initials;

position labels;

different shapes;

arrows with labels;

team names;

written notes;

clear verbal explanation.

Players should not have to decode colours before they can understand tactics.

What a Good Result Looks Like

A good greyscale result means the important items still separate clearly without colour.

For kits, the two teams should look different enough as full moving shapes. Shirts, shorts and socks should not collapse into one similar tone. Numbers should stand out. The goalkeeper and referee should be easy to separate from both teams.

For bibs, the groups should look clearly different when players move.

For cones, the marker should stand out from the playing surface.

For tactical boards, players should be able to understand the difference between teams, zones or movements without relying only on colour.

If the greyscale image is confusing, the real football picture may be confusing for some players too.

What to Do If the Greyscale Test Looks Poor

Do not panic. Fix what you can.

Possible actions:

change bib colours;

change shirts if spare kit is available;

add bibs if match rules allow;

change goalkeeper top if possible;

use stronger verbal communication;

tell players to call names earlier;

use numbers and positions;

use different cones;

move cones to a clearer background;

describe cones by position rather than colour;

add shapes or labels;

speak privately with any known colour-blind player;

warn the team that names and numbers matter today.

A poor greyscale result does not mean the game cannot happen. It means adults should add clearer cues before the confusion reaches the player.

Greyscale Test Script for Coaches

Before a match or session, a coach can say:

“We are going to check the picture quickly. If the colours are close, we will use names, numbers and positions more clearly.”

If players ask why, the coach can say:

“Good teams communicate clearly. We do not want anyone guessing.”

That answer protects dignity. It does not expose any player.

Greyscale Test Script for Parents

A parent can say to a coach:

“I took a quick greyscale photo and the kits look quite similar in tone. I know it is not a perfect test, but it may be worth encouraging names and numbers today.”

Or:

“These cones seem hard to see against the grass in greyscale. Could they be described by position as well as colour?”

Keep the tone practical, not accusatory.

Greyscale Test Script for Players

A player can say:

“Those kits look close to me. Could people call names early today?”

Or:

“That cone is hard for me to see quickly. Can we use cone numbers too?”

Or:

“If shirts are close, I am going to use socks, numbers and voices.”

This is not complaining. It is preparing to play better.

Limits of the Greyscale Test

The greyscale phone test has limits.

It does not diagnose colour blindness.

It does not tell you exactly how every colour-blind player will see the match.

It does not replace professional advice.

It does not guarantee that a kit is safe.

It does not replace governing-body rules or official kit clash procedures.

It can be affected by phone camera settings, screen brightness, weather, distance and lighting.

It is a practical warning tool, not a final answer.

Use it as one part of better football judgement.

The Main Question

After using the greyscale test, ask:

“Can the important information be understood without relying on colour alone?”

If yes, the setup is stronger.

If no, add another cue.

This is the purpose of the whole test. Not perfection. Not panic. Just better awareness before the mistake happens.

Appendix J — Equipment Colour Matrix

Football equipment should make the game clearer, not harder to read.

Every cone, bib, ball, line, magnet, marker and kit choice carries information. When that information is clear, players can focus on football. When it is unclear, some players may spend energy trying to work out what they are supposed to see before they can act.

This matrix is not a perfect scientific guide. Colour vision varies from player to player, lighting changes, surfaces change, and equipment looks different in movement than it does in a catalogue. The safest rule is always the same: do not rely on colour alone when the information matters.

Use this matrix as a practical starting point.

How to Use This Matrix

Before buying or using equipment, ask three questions.

First, does the item stand out from the background where it will actually be used?

A cone that looks bright indoors may not stand out on grass. A ball that looks clear in a shop may become harder to track on mud or under floodlights. A bib that looks different in the coach’s hand may not separate clearly once players are moving.

Second, does the item separate from other important colours in the session?

It is not enough for one bib to look bright. It has to look different from the other bibs. It is not enough for one team kit to look good alone. It has to separate from the opponent, goalkeeper, referee, pitch and background.

Third, is there another cue if colour fails?

Numbers, names, labels, shapes, sizes, positions, directions, textures and voice cues can all help. The best equipment is not only colourful. It is identifiable in more than one way.

General Colour Risk Guide

Some colour combinations are more likely to cause confusion for some colour-blind players, especially under speed, distance, mud, shadow or poor lighting.

Use extra caution with:

red and green;

red and orange;

green and yellow;

orange and green grass;

blue and purple;

dark blue and black;

dark green and black;

dark red and brown;

grey and pale colours;

dark against dark;

light against light;

bib colours that look different by name but similar in greyscale;

cones that sit close in tone to the playing surface;

goalkeeper or referee shirts that sit close to outfield kits.

This does not mean these colours can never be used. It means they should not be used as the only important cue without checking contrast and adding another identifier.

Bibs

Bibs are one of the easiest equipment choices to improve.

A good bib should stand out from the players’ training tops, the pitch surface, the other bib colour and the background. It should remain clear when players move, turn, sweat, get muddy or run through shadow.

Better bib practice:

Choose bibs with strong contrast between teams.

Avoid using red versus green as the main team split where possible.

Avoid using orange versus yellow or green versus yellow where possible.

Avoid blue versus purple where possible.

Avoid dark bibs over dark training tops.

Use one bib team and one non-bib team if that creates clearer separation.

Use numbered bibs where possible.

Use team names as well as colours.

Use direction of attack as a cue.

Check bibs in greyscale if unsure.

If bib colours are limited, the coach can still add clarity by saying, “Bibs attack the clubhouse, non-bibs attack the car park,” or “Jamie’s team press first,” rather than relying on colour alone.

Cones and Markers

Cones should be chosen for the surface, not just for brightness in a bag.

A red or orange cone may look obvious in the coach’s hand but less clear on grass for some players. Mud, leaves, shadow, worn artificial turf and indoor flooring can all change how a cone appears.

Better cone practice:

Use cones that strongly contrast with the playing surface.

Check cones from the player’s distance, not only close up.

Use cone numbers when possible.

Use different cone shapes or sizes.

Use tall cones for important targets.

Describe cones by position as well as colour.

Avoid making red or orange cones on grass the only key instruction.

Use white, blue or other high-contrast markers where they stand out better.

Check cones in greyscale if unsure.

Instead of saying, “Turn at the orange cone,” say, “Turn at cone two, nearest the goal.” That gives the player two routes to understanding.

Balls

The ball is the most important object on the pitch. It should be visible against the surface and lighting conditions.

A ball that is easy to see on a dry grass pitch may be less visible in mud, under floodlights, on an indoor floor or on artificial turf. Some patterned balls may also create confusion if the pattern reduces contrast.

Better ball practice:

Choose a ball that stands out from the surface.

Check visibility in real lighting conditions.

Consider whether mud will reduce contrast.

Avoid relying on colour patterns that blend into the pitch.

Check whether the ball is visible under floodlights.

Ask players if the ball is easy to track.

Use a different ball where possible if visibility is poor.

A player struggling to track the ball may not always say so. They may simply react late, misjudge pace or look less sharp.

Kits

Kits should be judged as full moving bodies, not as shirts on a hanger.

A shirt may look different from the opponent’s shirt but still become confusing when shorts, socks, mud, light, goalkeeper colours and referee colours are added. Players often see one another from the side, from behind or through peripheral vision.

Better kit practice:

Check shirts, shorts and socks together.

Check numbers for contrast.

Check both teams standing and moving.

Check from the touchline and from behind.

Check under the real lighting conditions.

Check whether mud may cover sock cues.

Check goalkeeper shirts against both teams.

Check referee shirts against both teams.

Use a greyscale photo if unsure.

Use spare kit or bibs where rules allow if the clash is serious.

A kit clash is not only a colour issue. It is a recognition issue. If players cannot recognise safe and unsafe shirts quickly, the game becomes less fair.

Goalkeeper Kit

The goalkeeper’s kit should be clearly separate from both teams and the referee.

Goalkeepers are often used as passing options and visual anchors. If the goalkeeper shirt blends with either team, confusion can appear in build-up play, set pieces, crowded penalty areas and quick transitions.

Better goalkeeper kit practice:

Check goalkeeper kit against both outfield teams.

Check goalkeeper kit against the referee.

Check visibility under light, shadow and mud.

Check from distance.

Keep a spare goalkeeper top where possible.

Make sure the goalkeeper communicates clearly if colours are close.

Use voice cues such as “keeper” and player names.

The goalkeeper should not become another confusing moving colour.

Referee Shirt

The referee is part of the moving picture.

A referee shirt that sits close to either team colour can create confusion, especially in peripheral vision. A player may glance quickly and mistake the referee for a passing option or opponent.

Better referee shirt practice:

Check referee shirt against both teams.

Check referee shirt against both goalkeepers.

Use an alternative referee top where available and appropriate.

Encourage players to use names and numbers if the referee shirt creates visual clutter.

Consider the referee’s position in crowded play.

This may seem small until a player makes a decision based on a quick visual glimpse.

Tactical Boards

Tactical boards often rely heavily on coloured magnets or dots. That can make them harder for some players to follow.

Better tactical board practice:

Use numbers on magnets.

Use player initials.

Use position names.

Use different shapes where possible.

Label arrows.

Use team names.

Avoid explaining movement through colour alone.

Check the board in greyscale if unsure.

Speak slowly enough for the player to connect the board to the pitch.

A player should be learning tactics, not decoding colours.

Training Apps and Digital Resources

Digital football tools can create the same problems as physical equipment.

Apps, diagrams, video analysis, heat maps, tactical graphics and training plans often rely on colour as the main meaning. Red means one team, blue means another. Green means success, orange means warning, red means error. Arrows, zones and overlays can become difficult if colour is the only cue.

Better digital practice:

Use labels as well as colours.

Use numbers and names.

Use patterns or shapes.

Avoid red/green-only success and error systems.

Add text explanations.

Check graphics in greyscale.

Make sure players can understand the information without colour alone.

A professional-looking graphic is not automatically accessible.

Use this as a quick reference.

Buying Equipment

When buying new equipment, do not only ask what looks bright.

Ask:

Will this stand out on our actual pitch?

Will this still be clear in mud, rain, shade or floodlights?

Does this colour work with the other colours we already own?

Can players identify it without relying only on colour?

Is there a number, label, shape or size difference?

Would this still be clear in greyscale?

Would this help a coach explain the session faster?

Would this protect a player who has not told us they are colour blind?

Better equipment decisions can prevent years of small confusion.

Low-Budget Options

Not every club can buy new equipment immediately. Clarity can still improve without large spending.

Low-budget options include:

Use one bib team and one non-bib team.

Use team names instead of colour names.

Use direction of attack as a cue.

Number cones with tape or marker.

Use different cone sizes.

Place cones near landmarks.

Use white tape or labels on cones.

Ask players to call names early.

Use socks, shorts or numbers as extra cues.

Take greyscale photos before matches.

Keep a written list of risky colour combinations.

Share a simple clarity checklist with coaches.

A lack of budget should not mean a lack of awareness.

Equipment Rule to Remember

If the equipment is important to the decision, it should be identifiable in more than one way.

Colour plus number.

Colour plus name.

Colour plus shape.

Colour plus position.

Colour plus direction.

Colour plus voice.

Colour plus contrast.

When the game is fast, one cue may not be enough for every player.

Final Reminder

Equipment is not neutral.

A bib can create clarity or confusion.

A cone can guide a player or disappear.

A kit can reveal teammates or hide them.

A tactical board can explain football or become another colour test.

The best equipment does not only look bright. It makes the game easier to understand quickly.

Make the equipment visible.

Then judge the football.

Appendix K — Club Colour Clarity Policy Starter

A club does not need to be perfect to start protecting players better.

It needs a clear standard.

A colour clarity policy is not a medical policy. It is not a diagnosis process. It is not a promise that every colour issue will be solved in every match. It is a practical club commitment to make important football information easier to see, understand and use.

The purpose is simple: no player should be judged unfairly because the game was relying on colour information they could not separate quickly enough.

This starter policy can be adapted by grassroots clubs, schools, academies, football programmes, tournaments and community organisations.

Club Colour Clarity Policy

Our club recognises that football uses colour as information. Kits, bibs, cones, balls, goalkeeper shirts, referee shirts, tactical boards, training zones, apps and coaching instructions can all affect how players understand the game.

We also recognise that some players may have colour vision deficiency, poor eyesight, blurred vision, light sensitivity or other visual needs. Some players may know this already. Some may not. Some may feel comfortable speaking about it. Some may prefer privacy.

Our aim is not to diagnose players. Our aim is to make football information clearer, protect player dignity and help coaches judge football ability more fairly.

We believe:

Colour should not be the only cue when important information must be understood quickly.

Players should not be publicly exposed because of colour vision or eyesight concerns.

Coaches should use clear communication, including names, numbers, positions, directions and shapes as well as colour.

Equipment choices should consider contrast and visibility.

Parents and players should have a private route to raise concerns.

Coaches should ask better questions before turning repeated mistakes into labels.

Clearer football helps everyone.

Core Principle

Before we blame the player, we check the picture.

This means that when a player repeatedly struggles with kits, bibs, cones, coloured zones, tactical boards or colour-based instructions, we will consider whether the visual setup may be part of the problem.

This does not mean every mistake is caused by colour vision.

This does not mean football standards are lowered.

This means we will make the game visible enough for standards to be fair.

Privacy and Dignity

Our club will handle colour vision and eyesight concerns privately and respectfully.

We will not announce a player’s colour blindness, eyesight issue or visual difficulty to teammates, parents or other players without appropriate consent.

We will avoid language that makes the player feel broken, weak, stupid, lazy or different.

Where possible, we will make general improvements for the whole group rather than singling out one player.

For example, instead of saying:

“We are changing this because this player cannot see colours,”

we may say:

“We are making the setup clearer for everyone.”

The goal is to protect the player while improving the environment.

Coaching Standards

Coaches should continue to coach football properly. Players should still be challenged to scan, pass, receive, defend, attack, communicate, compete, make decisions and improve.

However, coaches should also consider whether the visual information in the session is clear enough.

“Red team press.”

“Go to the orange cone.”

“Blue group rotate.”

“Defend the green gate.”

“Pass to yellow.”

colour plus name;

colour plus number;

colour plus direction;

colour plus position;

colour plus shape;

colour plus role;

colour plus landmark.

Examples:

“Red team, attacking the clubhouse, press.”

“Blue group, group one, rotate.”

“Go to cone number two, nearest the goal.”

“Defend the left-hand gate.”

“Pass to Jamie’s team.”

When buying, choosing or using equipment, the club will consider visibility and contrast.

bib colours;

cone colours;

ball visibility;

kit contrast;

sock and short contrast;

goalkeeper shirts;

referee shirts where relevant;

tactical board magnets;

digital training graphics;

pitch markings;

coloured zones.

red and green;

red and orange;

green and yellow;

blue and purple;

dark against dark;

light against light;

orange or red cones on grass;

goalkeeper shirts close to outfield kits;

referee shirts close to team kits.

Where ideal equipment is not available, coaches should add non-colour cues.

Coaches should consider:

whether bib colours separate at speed;

whether bibs separate in shade or floodlights;

whether bibs are worn over similar training tops;

whether red/green, blue/purple, orange/yellow or dark/dark combinations can be avoided;

whether numbered bibs are available;

whether teams can be identified by direction, name or role as well as colour.

Coaches should consider:

whether cones stand out on grass, mud, indoor floors or artificial turf;

whether cones remain visible from distance;

whether cones remain visible while a player is moving with the ball;

whether red or orange cones are being used on grass as the only important cue;

whether cone numbers, shapes, sizes or positions can be used.

“the cone nearest the goal”;

shirts;

shorts;

socks;

numbers;

goalkeeper shirts;

referee shirts where relevant;

Appendix L — School PE Guidance Sheet

School PE should be a place where children learn movement, confidence, teamwork and enjoyment. It should not become a hidden colour test.

Many PE lessons use colour without anyone thinking about it. Children are sent to coloured cones, divided into coloured bibs, asked to follow coloured zones, shown colour-coded diagrams, or told to stand on coloured lines. For most children, this may feel simple. For a child with colour vision deficiency, it may create confusion before the physical task even begins.

The child may not say anything. They may not know they are colour blind. They may know colour names but still struggle to use colour quickly. They may copy other children, wait to see where the group moves, avoid answering, guess, or accept being corrected. The teacher may see the hesitation but not the reason.

This guidance sheet is designed for teachers, PE staff, teaching assistants, coaches working in schools and school leaders.

The aim is not to diagnose children. The aim is to avoid relying on colour alone when instructions, safety or participation depend on it.

The Core Principle for Schools

If colour carries important information, add another cue.

Colour can still be used. It can be helpful, fast and familiar. But colour should not be the only way a child understands where to go, what team they are on, which equipment to use, which area is safe, or what task they are meant to complete.

Use colour plus:

words;

numbers;

shapes;

positions;

symbols;

labels;

landmarks;

directions;

names;

patterns;

verbal explanation.

A child should not have to reveal a private visual difficulty just to understand the lesson.

Why This Matters in PE

PE moves quickly. Children may be running, listening, changing direction, sharing equipment, competing, watching others and managing social pressure at the same time. If the instruction depends on colour alone, a colour-blind child may need extra time to work out what others receive instantly.

That extra time can look like poor listening. It can look like laziness. It can look like low confidence. It can look like a child who does not understand the rules. In reality, the child may be trying to decode the colour before they can join the activity.

This matters because PE is public. Mistakes happen in front of classmates. A child sent to the wrong cone or the wrong team can feel embarrassed. If it happens repeatedly, they may start avoiding PE, sport or team activities. The issue is not only access. It is confidence.

Common PE Colour Traps

Colour-only instructions can appear in many ordinary school activities.

Examples include:

“Run to the red cone.”

“Blue team starts.”

“Stand on the green line.”

“Collect the yellow ball.”

“Move into the orange zone.”

“Defend the purple gate.”

“Follow the red arrows.”

“Use the green bibs.”

“Go to the colour that matches your card.”

“Look at the blue route on the map.”

“Use the red markers for your group.”

“Stop when you reach the yellow area.”

These instructions may be clear for many children, but not for all. The safer version is to keep the colour and add another cue.

Better PE Instructions

Instead of saying:

“Run to the red cone,”

say:

“Run to the red cone nearest the goal.”

Instead of saying:

“Blue team starts,”

say:

“Blue team, the team by the wall, starts.”

Instead of saying:

“Stand on the green line,”

say:

“Stand on the green line closest to the door.”

Instead of saying:

“Collect the yellow ball,”

say:

“Collect the yellow ball in the middle basket.”

Instead of saying:

“Move into the orange zone,”

say:

“Move into the orange zone marked with number two.”

Instead of saying:

“Defend the purple gate,”

say:

“Defend the purple gate on the left.”

Instead of saying:

“Follow the red arrows,”

say:

“Follow the red arrows with the triangle symbol.”

This helps the whole class. Clearer instructions reduce repeated explanations, speed up transitions and support children who may be tired, distracted, anxious, new to English, younger, neurodivergent or visually unsure.

Cones, Lines and Zones

Cones are a common PE tool, but cone colour can be unreliable for some children. A red or orange cone on grass may be difficult. A green cone on grass may disappear. A pale cone on a light indoor floor may not stand out. A dark marker on a dark surface may be weak.

Better practice:

Use cones that contrast strongly with the surface.

Use different shapes or sizes where possible.

Number cones if they are important.

Put cones near landmarks.

Describe cones by position as well as colour.

Avoid making colour the only way to find a target.

Check the layout from the child’s distance, not only close up.

Think about shadows, low sun, indoor lighting and surface colour.

A child running with a ball should not have to search for a cone that the surface is hiding.

Bibs and Teams

Bibs can create confusion if teams are separated only by colour. Some bib combinations may be especially difficult, including red/green, red/orange, green/yellow, blue/purple, dark/dark or light/light combinations.

Better practice:

Use strong contrast between teams.

Use one bib team and one non-bib team if clearer.

Use team names as well as bib colours.

Use starting positions as cues.

Use numbers or group names.

Avoid publicly naming one child as the reason for changing bibs.

Tell children, “We are making the teams easier for everyone to recognise.”

Example:

“Bibs are Team A and will attack the playground end. Non-bibs are Team B and will attack the field end.”

This gives every child more than one route to understanding.

Balls and Equipment

Ball visibility matters. Some balls are hard to see against grass, mud, indoor floors or certain backgrounds. Patterned balls may also become harder to track if the pattern reduces contrast.

Better practice:

Choose balls that stand out against the surface.

Check visibility in the actual lesson area.

Avoid using equipment that blends into the background.

Label equipment boxes with words or symbols as well as colours.

Avoid colour-only storage systems.

Use signs, numbers or shapes on equipment areas.

A child who struggles to track a ball may not say, “I cannot see it clearly.” They may simply react late, miss catches or avoid involvement.

Classroom and PE Connection

Colour vision difficulty may appear outside PE too. A child may struggle with colour-coded maps, graphs, diagrams, charts, science work, art tasks or classroom labels. If a pupil has repeated difficulty with colour-coded learning materials and also struggles with PE colour cues, the pattern is worth noting.

Teachers should avoid assuming that correct colour naming always rules out colour vision difficulty. Some children learn colour names through memory and context. The important question is whether the child can use colour accurately and quickly when it carries meaning.

If concerns continue, schools can speak with parents or carers sensitively and suggest they seek advice from a qualified eye-care professional. Schools should not diagnose from classroom or PE behaviour.

Protecting the Child’s Dignity

Never embarrass a child for colour confusion.

Avoid saying things like:

“You know what colour that is.”

“Everyone else can see it.”

“Why did you go to the wrong cone?”

“It is obvious.”

“Are you not listening?”

These comments may make the child hide the problem. They may also turn a practical access issue into shame.

Better language:

“Let’s make the instruction clearer.”

“Use the cone nearest the goal.”

“Try the marker with the number two.”

“Stand with the team by the wall.”

“Thanks for telling me that colour is hard to see.”

“We will use names and numbers as well.”

The aim is to make clarity normal, not embarrassing.

If a Child Says Colours Are Difficult

If a child tells a teacher or coach that colours are difficult, respond calmly.

A useful response is:

“Thank you for telling me. That helps me explain things better. We can use numbers, positions and names as well as colours.”

Do not make a public announcement. Do not joke. Do not ask the child to prove it. Do not tell the class unless there is a clear, agreed reason and the child’s dignity is protected.

If the concern seems significant or repeated, speak privately with the appropriate school staff and the child’s parent or carer.

If a Parent Raises Colour Vision Concerns

If a parent tells the school their child may have colour vision difficulty, the response should be practical and respectful.

A useful school response is:

“Thank you for letting us know. We will avoid relying on colour alone where possible and will use labels, numbers, positions and clear verbal instructions. We will also keep this discreet and protect your child’s dignity.”

Schools should not treat this as an excuse to lower expectations. The child still participates, learns and improves. The difference is that important information is made clearer.

The goal is simple: within 30 days, the club should have clearer equipment habits, better coaching language, a private route for concerns, and a basic colour clarity standard that protects players without exposing them.

The 30-Day Aim

By the end of 30 days, the club should be able to say:

Week 1 — Notice the Picture

The first week is about awareness and audit. Do not start by buying new equipment or rewriting every policy. Start by looking at what the club already uses.

Choose one person to lead the review. This may be a welfare officer, coach coordinator, club secretary, head coach, academy lead, PE lead or parent volunteer. The person does not need to be an expert in colour vision. They need to be organised, practical and respectful of privacy.

Begin by checking the club’s current equipment. Lay out the bibs, cones, balls, tactical boards and any training markers. Look at them on the surface where they are actually used. A cone that looks bright in a storage room may not stand out on grass. A bib that looks different in a pile may not separate clearly when players move. A ball that looks visible indoors may be harder to see under floodlights or on mud.

Take photos. Use a greyscale phone check where useful. Look at whether colours still separate when colour is removed. This is not a formal test, but it can reveal low-contrast problems quickly.

During this first week, the club should ask:

Are our bib colours clear enough?

Do we use risky combinations such as red and green, blue and purple, green and yellow, red and orange, dark against dark, or light against light?

Do our cones stand out on grass, mud, artificial turf or indoor floors?

Do our coaches rely on colour-only instructions?

Do our tactical boards use numbers, initials or positions, or only coloured magnets?

Do our match-day kits create possible problems with goalkeeper shirts or referee shirts?

Do parents and players know how to raise a concern privately?

Would a colour-blind child in our club feel safe telling someone?

The aim of Week 1 is not to fix everything. The aim is to see the picture honestly.

Check cones against the actual playing surface.

Identify the highest-risk equipment combinations.

Agree that player privacy must be protected.

At the end of Week 1, the club should have a simple list of what is clear, what is risky, and what can be improved quickly.

Week 2 — Change the Coaching Language

Week 2 is about coaching habits.

Most clubs can improve colour clarity immediately by changing the way instructions are given. This costs nothing. It does not require new equipment. It does not reduce standards. It simply gives players more than one route to understanding.

The rule for Week 2 is:

Use colour plus another cue.

Instead of saying only, “Red team press,” coaches should say, “Red team, attacking the clubhouse, press.”

Instead of saying only, “Go to the orange cone,” coaches should say, “Go to the orange cone nearest the goal,” or “Go to cone number two.”

Instead of saying only, “Blue group rotate,” coaches should say, “Blue group, group one, rotate.”

Instead of saying only, “Defend the green gate,” coaches should say, “Defend the left-hand gate.”

This habit helps colour-blind players, but it also helps everyone. Younger players understand faster. Tired players make fewer mistakes. Players who missed the first instruction have another cue. Teams communicate better. Sessions become cleaner.

Coaches should also begin using the question, “What did you see?” when mistakes look unusual or repeated. This question should be asked privately where possible. It is not a replacement for coaching. It is a way to understand whether the mistake was football, information, or both.

A club can introduce this in a short coach message:

“From this week, please avoid relying on colour alone in important instructions. Use colour plus another cue: number, name, position, direction, shape or landmark. If a player repeatedly struggles with a colour-coded task, ask privately what they saw before judging the mistake.”

This simple change can alter the culture quickly.

Week 3 is about communication and dignity.

Players and parents need a safe way to raise concerns. If the only option is to shout across the pitch or speak in front of other parents, many families will stay silent. If players think they will be teased, they may hide the issue. If parents worry they will be accused of making excuses, they may avoid the conversation until confidence has already been damaged.

A club should create a simple private route.

a coach coordinator;

a private email address;

a short online form;

a conversation with the team coach;

a parent liaison person;

a confidential note to the club secretary.

The route does not need to be complicated. It needs to be clear.

Parents should be able to say:

“My child may have difficulty with certain colours. We are not asking for lower standards, but names, numbers and clearer cues help.”

Players should be able to say:

“Thank you for telling us. We do not diagnose players, but we can make sessions clearer and keep this discreet. We can use names, numbers, positions and directions as well as colour. If you have concerns about colour vision or eyesight, please speak to a qualified eye-care professional.”

This route should also remind families that a standard eyesight check and a colour vision test are not always the same thing. The club should not give medical advice. It should point families towards proper professional support.

Tell parents and players how to raise concerns discreetly.

Create a short club message explaining the route.

Remind staff and coaches that concerns must be handled respectfully.

Make clear that the club does not diagnose.

Agree that no player should be publicly identified without appropriate consent.

At the end of Week 3, the club should have a way for concerns to be raised before they become regret.

The club should now have an equipment audit, clearer coaching language, and a private communication route. The next step is to write these into a short policy or guidance note that coaches, parents and volunteers can understand.

The policy does not need to be long. It should say what the club believes, what coaches should do, how concerns are handled, and what practical habits the club will use.

The club does not have to buy everything at once. The key is to stop making equipment choices without considering visibility.

Source Notes and Further Reading

This section is included to help readers understand where the book’s main evidence points come from, which claims are well supported, and which parts of the book are practical interpretation rather than settled football-specific research.

The book uses careful language because colour blindness in football is both real and under-discussed. Some facts are well established, such as the existence of colour vision deficiency, its higher prevalence in males, and the need not to rely on colour alone when information matters. Other parts of the book, such as the emotional cost of late discovery, the worry carried by players, and the possible effect on football opportunity, are practical and human concerns that deserve attention but should not be overstated as proven causes of failed careers.

The purpose of the evidence is not to turn every mistake into colour blindness. The purpose is to show that the question is serious enough to ask earlier.

1. What Colour Vision Deficiency Means

The NHS describes colour vision deficiency, commonly called colour blindness, as seeing colours differently from most people and having difficulty telling colours apart. The NHS also notes that colour vision deficiency can be present from birth or begin at any age, and that inherited colour vision deficiency currently has no treatment, though many people adapt to living with it. (nhs.uk)

This book uses the term colour blindness because it is widely understood by the public, but it also uses colour vision deficiency because that phrase is more precise. Most colour-blind people do not see only in black and white. Many see colour, but some colours may be harder to tell apart or may not be reliable enough under speed, pressure, distance, mud, shade, floodlights or movement.

The football meaning is simple: the issue is not always whether a player can name a colour while standing still. The issue is whether the player can separate important colour information quickly enough during play.

2. Testing: Eyesight and Colour Vision Are Not the Same Thing

A key practical point in the book is that a normal eyesight check and colour vision testing should not be treated as the same thing. The NHS lists Ishihara plates and colour arrangement tests as two main tests for colour vision deficiency, and states that colour vision testing is not part of the routine NHS eye test, but can be requested from an optician if needed. (nhs.uk)

This is why the book repeatedly advises parents not to diagnose from football behaviour, but to speak to a qualified eye-care professional and ask specifically about colour vision testing where there are repeated concerns.

The book also mentions eyesight issues such as blurred vision, headaches, light sensitivity, poor eyesight, glasses, sports goggles and contact lenses. These are included to remind readers that football vision is not only about colour. A player may need colour vision checked, eyesight checked, or both. This book does not prescribe a solution. It encourages proper professional advice.

3. Common Prevalence Figures

A widely used public rule of thumb is that colour vision deficiency affects around 1 in 12 males and around 1 in 200 females. Colour Blind Awareness gives this estimate as approximately 1 in 12 men, or 8%, and 1 in 200 women, and also estimates about 3 million colour-blind people in the UK and around 300 million worldwide. (Colour Blind Awareness)

This book uses those figures carefully. They are useful for explaining why football should expect colour-blind players to exist in schools, clubs and squads, especially in boys’ and men’s football. They should not be read as exact counts of players in any one team.

The book also makes clear that girls can be colour blind too. The prevalence is lower in females, but the practical football barrier is not smaller when a girl is affected.

4. The 2025 Paediatric Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

The book refers to a 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis titled Global Prevalence of Congenital Color Vision Deficiency among Children and Adolescents, 1932–2022, published in Ophthalmology. The study analysed data from 56 studies, with 1,703,619 individuals across 21 countries and 5 continents. It estimated global congenital colour vision deficiency prevalence among children and adolescents at 2.59%, with higher prevalence in males, 4.38%, than females, 0.64%. (ScienceDirect)

This book uses that study as an important modern prevalence reference, while still making clear that it is not a football-specific count. It helps show scale. It does not prove how many footballers are affected, how many players have been misjudged, or how many opportunities have been changed.

The correct wording is: based on the available paediatric prevalence research, colour vision deficiency is common enough that football should not treat it as a rare curiosity.

5. Global Child-Population Estimate

The book includes a rough global scale estimate that around 60–63 million children worldwide may have congenital colour vision deficiency if a 2.59% pooled prevalence estimate is applied to global child-population figures. UNICEF Data lists the world population under age 18 at about 2.415 billion children. (UNICEF DATA)

The calculation is simple: 2.415 billion multiplied by 2.59% gives roughly 62.5 million.

This is why the book labels the figure as a scale estimate, not an official count. It should not be presented as a UNICEF colour-blindness statistic. It is an author calculation using UNICEF’s under-18 population figure and the 2025 prevalence estimate.

The safe wording is: roughly 60–63 million children worldwide may have congenital colour vision deficiency, based on applying the 2.59% pooled prevalence figure to global under-18 population estimates.

6. UK Under-18 Estimate

The book also includes a rough UK under-18 estimate. UNICEF Data lists the UK population under age 18 at about 14,403,544 in 2023. (UNICEF DATA)

Using sex-specific rule-of-thumb assumptions, such as around 8% of males and 0.5% of females, a rough estimate of UK under-18s affected by colour vision deficiency comes out at about 600,000-plus children, depending on the exact age and sex distribution used. This book’s earlier working estimate of roughly 634,000–636,000 should be labelled clearly as an author estimate, not an official count.

The safest wording is: using UK under-18 population estimates and common sex-specific prevalence assumptions, hundreds of thousands of UK under-18s may be affected. If a precise number is used, it should be marked as an estimate and recalculated during final publication using the latest official population table.

7. Classroom and Squad Rules of Thumb

The book refers to the idea that there may be at least one colour-blind child in an average co-educational classroom, and at least one colour-blind player in a typical male football squad. These are rules of thumb, not census facts.

They come from applying common prevalence estimates to ordinary group sizes. If around 1 in 12 males are colour blind, a boys’ football squad, academy age group or large training group may statistically include one or more affected players. If mixed classrooms include both boys and girls, the likelihood depends on class size and sex mix.

These statements should be used to make adults alert, not to claim certainty about every classroom or team.

Safe wording: in many ordinary school or football settings, it is reasonable to expect that colour vision deficiency may already be present, whether diagnosed or not.

8. Football Guidance from Colour Blind Awareness, The FA and UEFA

The book’s football-specific practical advice is strongly influenced by existing football guidance produced by Colour Blind Awareness in conjunction with The FA and UEFA. Colour Blind Awareness hosts guidance documents for football, including The FA/UEFA Colour Blindness in Football guidance document, UEFA advice for coach educators, and advice when selecting kit colours. The guidance is designed to help football stakeholders understand how colour blindness can affect many aspects of football. (Colour Blind Awareness)

The FA has also publicly described its colour blindness guidance as explaining different types of colour blindness, answering common questions and offering positive interventions for people who may be affected. In that context, former Norway national team manager Lars Lagerbäck is quoted as saying that when play moves quickly, it is easy to end up in situations where it is hard to tell whether another player is an opponent or teammate. (www.thefa.com)

This supports one of the book’s central football claims: colour confusion matters more when the game is moving quickly.

9. Kit Clashes, Socks, Pitch Colour and Lighting

UEFA’s kit-selection advice notes that kit clarity can be affected by lighting conditions, including movement from shade into sunlight and floodlighting. It also warns that all-red, all-orange and all-green kits can be difficult to distinguish from the colour of the pitch, and that some sock colours can “disappear” against the pitch, affecting colour-blind players’ ability to distinguish players. (UEFA.com)

This source supports the book’s repeated instruction to check the whole kit, not only the shirt. Shirts, shorts, socks, numbers, goalkeeper kit, referee kit, mud, shade, low sun and floodlights can all affect the moving picture.

The book’s advice to check kits in greyscale is a practical extension of the contrast principle. It should be described as a useful warning tool, not as a formal or official test.

10. Referee Kits and Peripheral Vision

The FA’s referee guidance notes that problems can arise if colour-blind players cannot distinguish between the referee’s kit and either team’s kit. It also notes that players with red vision deficiency commonly report difficulty when their team plays in all red and match officials are in all black, especially when trying to understand kit colours in peripheral vision while receiving or passing. (www.thefa.com)

This supports the book’s inclusion of referee shirts and peripheral vision as practical match-day issues. A colour-blind player may not be looking at a perfect front-on view. They may be catching a quick glimpse while turning, scanning, receiving or passing.

11. Coaching, Kit and Equipment

UEFA’s football coach factsheet includes practical suggestions such as ensuring match kits are not colour-blind kit clashes, using blue versus yellow or white bibs as first choice in training, using white or yellow line markings as first choice and avoiding red, and using blue versus white or yellow magnets for outfield players in tactical training. (UEFA.com)

Colour Blind Awareness also states that it has created football guidance documents with The FA and UEFA, including coach factsheets and kit-colour advice. (Colour Blind Awareness)

These sources support the book’s practical message: coaches can often help by changing bibs, cones, line colours, tactical-board design and coaching language before players are judged.

12. Do Not Rely on Colour Alone

The book’s repeated phrase “use more than colour” is also supported by established accessibility guidance. The W3C Web Accessibility Initiative explains WCAG Success Criterion 1.4.1, “Use of Color,” as requiring that colour not be used as the only visual means of conveying information, indicating an action, prompting a response or distinguishing a visual element. The guidance explains that if information is conveyed through colour differences, users with colour deficiencies may not see it, so the information should also be provided through another visual means. (W3C)

Although WCAG is web accessibility guidance, the principle transfers well to football: if colour carries important information, add another cue.

In football, the additional cue might be a name, number, position, direction, shape, symbol, landmark, voice cue, sock cue, short cue, pattern or tactical label.

13. School and Learning Effects

The NHS notes that colour vision deficiency may affect a child’s performance at school and that schools may be able to make adjustments for lessons and exams. (nhs.uk)

This supports the book’s inclusion of school PE, classroom charts, maps, graphs, diagrams and colour-coded learning materials. The book should not claim that colour blindness always affects school performance. The safer point is that colour-coded school tasks can create avoidable barriers if colour is the only cue.

14. What the Book Adds Beyond Existing Guidance

Existing sources cover colour vision deficiency, common prevalence estimates, testing, school effects, accessibility principles and football kit/coaching guidance. This book builds on those foundations by focusing specifically on the emotional and practical pathway for youth football players, parents and coaches.

The book’s original emphasis includes:

the player’s hidden worry during matches;

decision fatigue from constant colour-checking;

paradox of choice when the player must verify too many cues;

the parent’s financial and emotional investment in the football dream;

the regret of late discovery;

the risk that a player may be labelled before the visual picture is checked;

the need to protect privacy and dignity;

the idea that football should reveal ability, not hide it behind avoidable visual confusion.

These points are practical interpretations and lived-experience concerns. They are not presented as proven causal pathways for professional success or failure. They are included because they are plausible, football-specific and important enough for adults to consider.

15. Evidence-Safe Limits

This book deliberately avoids certain claims.

It does not claim that colour blindness causes failed football careers.

It does not claim that every colour-blind player struggles.

It does not claim that every wrong-team pass is caused by colour confusion.

It does not claim that fixing colour cues guarantees success.

It does not claim an exact number of footballers affected.

It does not claim a universal measured delay for all colour-blind players.

It does not claim that coaches, parents or clubs are to blame for every missed case.

The book’s strongest evidence-safe claim is this:

Football uses colour as information, and some players may not be able to use that information as quickly or reliably as others. Therefore, football should avoid relying on colour alone when important information must be understood quickly.

That claim is practical, proportionate and supported by both colour vision guidance and accessibility principles.

Final Call to Action

A child may be misunderstood this weekend.

Not because the adults around them do not care. Not because the coach is cruel. Not because the parent is blind to the child’s struggle. Not because the player is weak. But because football moves quickly, mistakes look obvious from the outside, and hidden visual confusion is easy to miss.

The next wrong pass may look like poor awareness.

The next hesitation may look like fear.

The next missed cone may look like poor listening.

The next safe pass may look like lack of bravery.

The next quiet car journey may look like disappointment after a normal bad game.

Maybe that is all it is.

But maybe there is another question to ask first.

What did you see?

That question is where change begins.

If you are a player, do not let one unclear picture become your whole identity. You are not one wrong pass. You are not one moment of hesitation. You are not lazy because some colours are harder. You are not stupid because a cone disappeared into the grass. You are not weak because you worry about passing to the wrong player. You still have to train, listen, compete, communicate and improve, but you deserve to understand the game you are being asked to read. Learn your cues. Ask for names, numbers and clearer information. Use your voice when you can. Find the next clear cue.

If you are a parent, start noticing patterns calmly. Do not panic. Do not diagnose from the touchline. Do not blame yourself for what you did not know. Ask your child what they saw. Watch what happens with kits, bibs, cones, light, mud, socks, shorts, numbers and confidence. If the pattern continues, speak to a qualified eye-care professional and ask specifically about colour vision testing. A standard eyesight check and a colour vision test are not always the same thing. Then speak to the coach privately, practically and respectfully. You are not making excuses. You are protecting understanding.

If you are a coach, check the picture before you label the player. Use colour, but do not rely on colour alone. Add names, numbers, positions, directions, shapes and voice. Check bibs. Check cones. Check kits. Use greyscale photos when useful. Ask, “What did you see?” before deciding the mistake was only attitude, bravery, focus or football intelligence. Keep standards high, but make the information clear enough for those standards to be fair. Coach the game, not the confusion.

If you are a club, school or academy, do not wait for one child to be embarrassed before you act. Make colour clarity part of normal practice. Create a private route for parents and players to raise concerns. Audit your bibs, cones, balls and tactical boards. Train coaches and teachers to use more than colour. Protect children’s dignity. Build a system where support does not depend on luck.

If you are a sponsor, supporter, business, optician, foundation, club partner or community organisation, help this message reach the people who need it. Fund books. Fund checklists. Fund digital games. Fund school packs. Fund coach resources. Fund clearer equipment. Fund the conversation before regret arrives. A sponsored copy of this book could help one parent ask a better question, one coach change a session, one player feel understood, and one child avoid years of unnecessary shame.

The work does not need to be perfect to begin.

Change the next instruction.

Change the next bib choice.

Change the next cone setup.

Change the next coach question.

Change the next parent conversation.

Change the next club policy.

Change the next child’s experience.

Football will always be hard. It should be. The game should challenge courage, skill, speed, decision-making, movement, communication and intelligence. But it should not hide a player’s ability behind avoidable visual confusion and then call the confusion weakness.

The final message of this book is simple enough to remember and important enough to repeat:

Before you blame the player, check the picture.

Make the game visible.

Then judge the football.
